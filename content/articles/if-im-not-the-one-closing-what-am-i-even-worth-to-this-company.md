You've created the space the company has been starving for.</p><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">What You're Actually Worth Now</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Let's answer the question directly. If you're not the one closing every deal, what are you worth to this company?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">More than you were when you were closing every deal. And here's why.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">You're worth the strategic accounts only a founder can land</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">There are five to ten accounts in your market where the buyer will not take the meeting unless you're on the call. Some of those are enterprise. Some are partnerships. Some are board-level introductions that turn into the next million in ARR. Those are yours. Not because you can't delegate them, but because they're the ones where the founder seat is the asset.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">You're worth the message your team can't write yet</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The reason you close at a higher rate than everyone else isn't magic. It's that you know what to say because you've heard every objection a thousand times. That knowledge needs to come out of your head and into a Sales Playbook and an Accountabilities Document your team can actually run. The day you do that, your value multiplies, because every rep is now selling like a slightly worse version of you instead of a much worse version of someone else.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">You're worth the hires nobody else can pick</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Your fingerprint on a hiring decision is worth more than your fingerprint on a discovery call. The reps you pick, the leader you bring in, the first marketing hire who finally produces real pipeline. Those decisions compound for years. The deal you close this Tuesday compounds for a quarter.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">You're worth the capital you can raise</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A founder who is the entire sales motion is hard to fund. The first question any serious investor asks is what happens to the business when you step away. If the honest answer is "it stops," your valuation has a ceiling and you know what it is. The company is only worth as much as the parts of it that aren't you.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(242, 103, 78); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">"Your real worth to the company is what you build, not what you close this quarter. I won't pretend that's a fix. It isn't. Most $1M to $10M ARR companies aren't ready for a full-time VP. They're ready for a system, and someone who can build it.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Here's the sequence that actually works.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">1. Write down what's in your head</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A Sales Playbook. ICP, qualifying questions, objection responses, pricing logic, the exact words you say at the close. Not a PDF you put on a shelf. A living document the team actually opens before every call.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">2. Define the role you're hiring into</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Not a job description. An Accountabilities Document. Daily activity expectations, weekly outputs, quarterly outcomes, and the specific metrics that will be reviewed every Monday. If you can't tell a candidate what success looks like at 30, 60, and 90 days, you're not ready to hire.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">3. Run the system before you scale it</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Two reps, not one. Always two. So you can compare, you can isolate the variable, and you can survive the one who doesn't work out. This is where most founders cut a corner that costs them six months and $200K.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">4. They write the Playbook, hire the team, run the cadence, and hand it off to a full-time VP once the motion is repeatable and the company can actually keep one busy.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: What if my identity really is wrapped up in being the closer?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Mine was too.
