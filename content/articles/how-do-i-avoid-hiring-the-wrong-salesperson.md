If you don't fix the why, the questions won't save you.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Founders make three predictable mistakes when they hire salespeople. I've made all three myself. So have you.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><b>Mistake one. You hire the person you wanted to be.</b> Most founders who came up through sales hire reps who remind them of a younger version of themselves. Same energy. Same confidence. Same instinct for the deal. That's not a candidate evaluation. That's a mirror. And mirrors don't tell you anything about whether the person can sell into your market.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><b>Mistake two. You hire the logo.</b> Big-name companies on the resume become a substitute for actual proof. The candidate sold at Salesforce for four years. Cool. What did they actually do at Salesforce? Did they ride inbound leads off a famous brand and a marketing engine that pre-qualified every conversation? Because that's a very different skill than building a pipeline from cold at a company nobody's heard of yet.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><b>Mistake three. You hire from the gut after a great conversation.</b> The room felt good. The candidate laughed at your jokes. You vibed. None of that predicts performance. It predicts whether you'll enjoy the first six months. Which is also when you'll be writing the severance package because you can't hold someone accountable on a number when every weekly one-on-one feels like a conversation between friends.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(224, 161, 38); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">"Founders don't hire the wrong salesperson because they're bad judges of people. Listen. Don't rescue them when the silence gets long.</text></svg></div><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Question 1. The Math: "Walk me through your numbers for the last four quarters."</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">This is the first question because it ends 30 percent of interviews on the spot. I want quota, attainment, average deal size, sales cycle length, and win rate. Four quarters. Off the top of their head.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Real salespeople know their numbers the way a baseball player knows their batting average. They live in them. When a candidate fumbles, hedges, or asks if they can "follow up with that information later," you've learned everything you need to know. They didn't own their pipeline at their last job. Somebody else owned it for them. They were a passenger.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">What you want to hear: "I carried a $1.2M quota, hit 117% last year, 94% the year before. Average deal was $42K, sales cycle around 75 days, win rate of 28%." Specific. Confident. Not rehearsed, lived.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">What you want to walk away from: "It varied a lot quarter to quarter" or "Our team did really well." The pronoun shift from "I" to "we" when you ask for personal numbers is a tell I've seen a thousand times.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Question 2. The Process: "Walk me through how you sold your last deal."</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Pick a specific deal. Make them walk you through it end to end. First touch. Discovery. Proposal. Objections. Close. Implementation handoff. Five to ten minutes, all of it.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">You're listening for two things. Does the candidate have a repeatable process they can describe? And does that process include the parts that are actually hard? Cold outreach if you're going to need it. Multi-threading the deal across multiple decision-makers. Handling a procurement curveball at the end. Negotiating without burning the price.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The candidates who can't do this will tell you about the deal at the customer level. "Yeah, they were a great prospect, we just clicked, we got it done." That's not a process. That's a love story. You can't replicate it at your company because you don't know what they actually did.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The candidates you want will walk you through it like they're teaching it to someone. Because they've taught it before. To themselves, every time they had to do it again from scratch.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Question 3. The Loss: "Tell me about the deal that hurt the most to lose."</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">This is the question that separates owners from blamers. Listen to the first ten seconds of their answer. If they start with the customer ("they ghosted us") or the company ("our product wasn't quite there") or the team ("legal took too long"), you've got a blamer. They will run the same playbook on your deals when they don't close.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">What you want to hear is "I." "I should have caught that earlier in discovery." "I didn't multi-thread to the CFO until week six and by then it was too late." "I let the deal stall for two weeks because I didn't want to push when they went quiet, and that was on me."</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A salesperson who can describe exactly how they lost a deal and what they'd do differently is a salesperson who can be coached. A salesperson who can't is a salesperson who'll be sitting across from you in nine months explaining why missing quota wasn't really their fault.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(224, 161, 38); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">"You hire the answer to question three. Everything else is paperwork. If they can't own a loss in an interview when they're trying to impress you, they'll never own one on your team."</blockquote><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Question 4. The Change: "What's the last thing you changed in how you sell, and why?"</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">This is the coaching question hidden inside a craft question. You're not asking if they're coachable directly. You're asking if they've ever changed.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The right answer is specific and recent. "Six months ago I started running discovery in two calls instead of one because I was getting to proposal too fast and losing on fit." Or, "Last quarter I stopped sending decks before the first meeting. I was getting screened out by procurement before I could explain the value."</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The wrong answer sounds like "I've kind of always done it the same way and it works." That candidate has stopped learning. At $1M to $10M ARR you can't afford a rep who has finished growing. Your market is moving too fast and your product is changing every quarter. You need a rep who'll change with it.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Bonus tell. Ask who taught them to make the change. If the answer is a manager, a coach, a peer, or a book, they're a learner. If they can't name anyone, they're not.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Question 5. The Why: "Why this role, why now, why us?"</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Three parts. Make them answer all three. This is the question that prevents the hire who'll leave in eleven months for a slightly better offer.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">"Why this role" tells you whether they understand what they're signing up for. A founder-led sales environment isn't a place. It's a stage. There's no marketing engine yet. There's no team to lean on. The candidate has to want that or they'll resent it by month four.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">"Why now" tells you what's actually driving the move. The honest answer is usually some version of "I need money" or "I'm bored" or "my last boss was awful." All three are okay answers. What's not okay is no answer, because that's the candidate who's interviewing everywhere and will take whatever lands first.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">"Why us" tells you whether they did the homework. If they can't articulate something specific about your company, your market, or your problem, they didn't prepare. Salespeople who don't prepare for an interview don't prepare for discovery calls either.</p><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">A Story From My Own Hiring Scars</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Years back, at MindIQ, I hired a candidate named Tom. He'd done seven years at a big enterprise software company. Had a great resume. Strong references. Interviewed beautifully. Funny, sharp, easy to talk to. I closed the offer before I'd asked him questions three or four.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Tom started. For about six weeks he was great in meetings, contributed in pipeline reviews, and built a list of accounts that all looked plausible on paper. Then nothing happened.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">He couldn't prospect. He'd never had to. At the big company he'd been handed a territory full of warm logos and an SDR team that built his pipeline for him. He didn't know how to cold call. He didn't know how to write an outbound email that got a response. He had no muscle for the part of the job that mattered most at our stage.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">If I'd asked him to walk me through how he sourced his last deal, I'd have caught it in twenty minutes. I didn't ask. I'd already made the hire in my head and was just running through interview motions to feel diligent. Tom and I parted ways six months in. The total cost, between salary, draw, ramp, and lost pipeline, was around $230,000. That doesn't include the customer relationships that stalled because nobody was actively working those accounts while Tom was theoretically working them.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(224, 161, 38); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">"I didn't hire Tom and find out he couldn't prospect. I hired Tom and chose not to find out. The interview is the cheapest place to be wrong about somebody."</blockquote><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">What Great Answers Look Like vs What to Walk Away From</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">After you've asked the five questions, sit down with your notes and put each answer in one of two columns. You're not scoring the candidate. You're forcing yourself to look at the pattern instead of the personality.</p><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 0px; border-radius: 10px; overflow: hidden; box-shadow: rgba(27, 67, 50, 0.1) 0px 4px 20px;"><svg viewBox="0 0 720 380" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" style="width:100%;display:block;"><text x="360" y="38" text-anchor="middle" font-family="Arial, sans-serif" font-size="15" font-weight="700" fill="#1B4332">Hire This Pattern vs Walk Away From This Pattern</text><text x="360" y="58" text-anchor="middle" font-family="Arial, sans-serif" font-size="11" fill="#4F6B5B" letter-spacing="0.03em">SAME FIVE QUESTIONS. They need at least four out of five to be in the right column, and the one that's borderline has to be something you can teach. You can teach process. You can teach product. You cannot teach ownership and you cannot teach work ethic. If those columns are weak, walk away no matter how much you like the person.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">The Accountabilities Document Comes Before the Interview</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">None of these five questions matter if you can't tell the candidate exactly what success looks like in the role. That's what the Accountabilities Document is. The number they'll carry. The activities they'll be measured on weekly. The decision rights they have. The decision rights they don't. The cadence they'll be coached on. The behaviors that get them fired.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">If you can't write that document before you post the job, you're not ready to hire. You're shopping. Hiring without an Accountabilities Document is how founders end up with a salesperson who is technically employed and structurally useless. The candidate doesn't know what they're being measured on. You don't know what you'd hold them to. So nobody holds anyone to anything and twelve months disappear.</p><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">How a Fractional Sales Leader Runs the Interview Without the Founder Rose-Colored Glasses</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Here's the part most founders underestimate. The five questions work. But the five questions only work if the interviewer is willing to ask them, sit through the awkward silence when a candidate fumbles, and write down the real answer instead of the one they wanted to hear.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">That's hard for a founder. You're charming yourself. The candidate is charming you. You're both selling to each other. You've got a hundred other fires you're trying to put out and a real desire to believe this hire will fix the pipeline problem so you can stop running discovery calls yourself. The room wants to close the deal even when the answers say not to.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A Fractional Sales Leader sits in the room on your side of the table. Asks the questions. Watches the answers. Catches the pronoun shifts, the hedges, the "we" where there should be an "I." Writes the Accountabilities Document with you so you both know what you're hiring against. And tells you the truth when the candidate you love isn't the candidate you should hire.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">What changes inside the first 90 days</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The Accountabilities Document gets written. The job description gets rewritten so it actually attracts your ICP candidate instead of every salesperson with a Calendly link. The interview process gets standardized. Every candidate gets the same five questions in the same order so you're comparing answers, not personalities. References get called, and the right questions get asked, not the polite ones. By day ninety, you've made a hire you're confident in or you've stopped looking because you know the pipeline isn't ready for one yet.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">What the founder gets back</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">You stop spending Saturday mornings interviewing candidates who looked great on LinkedIn and bombed in week one. You stop writing severance checks for hires you shouldn't have made. You start building a sales team you can actually scale instead of cycling through reps who couldn't survive your sales motion. The Fractional Sales Leader handles the recruiting work. You keep being the founder.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><a href="https://louiebernstein.com/articles/the-5-interview-questions-that-separate-real-salespeople-from-great-storytellers" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 20px 24px; border-radius: 8px; display: flex; align-items: center; gap: 12px; text-decoration-line: none; background: rgb(244, 246, 250); border-left: 4px solid rgb(27, 67, 50);"><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;"><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 4px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700; letter-spacing: 0.1em; text-transform: uppercase; display: block; color: rgb(27, 67, 50);">Related Reading</span><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.35; color: rgb(27, 67, 50);">The 5 Interview Questions That Separate Real Salespeople From Great Storytellers &rarr;</span></div></a><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">Frequently Asked Questions</h2><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How many interview rounds do I really need?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Three. A first screen on the phone to make sure the basics line up. A deep interview where you ask the five questions and dig into the answers. A working session where you give them a real exercise. Roleplay a discovery call with you as the prospect. Have them walk you through how they'd approach your first thirty days. Three rounds is enough to catch the wrong hire. More than three rounds usually means you don't have a process. You have anxiety.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: Should I roleplay a sales call with the candidate?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Yes. Always. You'll learn more in a 15-minute roleplay than in three rounds of conversational interviews. Give them a one-paragraph scenario about a fictional prospect. You play the prospect. They run discovery. Watch what they ask. Watch how they respond to a hesitation or a price pushback. Real salespeople love this. The wrong hire will resist it, hedge, or try to convert it into another conversation about themselves. Don't ask "was she great to work with?" You'll get a yes every time. Ask specific questions. "What was her quota and did she hit it?" "What did she struggle with?" "Would you hire her again into a $1M to $10M ARR company where she'd have to build pipeline from cold?" References will tell you the truth if you give them a specific question to answer. They'll tell you nothing if you give them an open-ended one.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: What if I'm interviewing my first sales hire and I've never managed a salesperson before?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Then the risk of hiring wrong is the highest it'll ever be, and the cost is the highest too. A founder hiring their first sales rep solo is the most common way I see $250,000 disappear at $1M to $10M ARR. The five questions help. The Accountabilities Document helps more. But honestly, this is the moment to bring in someone who has hired sales reps before. Don't learn this by losing six months and a quarter-million dollars when you can borrow the experience for a fraction of the cost.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: What if I love the candidate but the answers were weak?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Pass. I've watched founders override the five questions because the chemistry was good, and I've watched those same hires fail nine months later. The room feels good in an interview because that's the salesperson's job. Their job is to make rooms feel good. Yours is to find out if they can actually sell. If the answers don't hold up, the chemistry won't save you.
