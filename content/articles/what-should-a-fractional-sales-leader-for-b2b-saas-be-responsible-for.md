But a full-time VP of Sales costs $250K or more, and you've heard how those stories end. So you start looking at fractional sales leadership, and you run straight into the question nobody answers clearly: what exactly should this person be responsible for?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">I've seen the role defined as everything from "closes deals for me" to "gives me advice on Zoom once a week." Both are wrong. And both will cost you a year you don't have.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">So here's the direct answer. A Fractional Sales Leader for B2B SaaS should be responsible for <strong style="box-sizing: border-box;">building and running your sales system</strong>: one documented sales process, a hiring system, a weekly coaching rhythm, pipeline truth, compensation and quota math, and accountability in writing. They should not be responsible for being your quota-carrying rep, generating your leads, or fixing your product.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">That's the whole article in two sentences. The rest is the evidence, the specifics, and the line you should draw in writing before you sign anything.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-width: 1px medium medium; border-style: solid none none; border-color: rgb(226, 230, 237) currentcolor currentcolor; border-image: initial;"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">The Short Answer: They Own the System, Not the Selling</h2><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">Outcomes, not hours</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Fractional means part-time hours, usually 10 to 15 a week. What makes it work isn't the hours. It's that the engagement is defined by outcomes. A rep is responsible for a quota. A consultant is responsible for a report. A <a href="https://louiebernstein.com/articles/what-fractional-sales-leader-does" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 600; text-decoration: underline; text-underline-offset: 2px; color: rgb(14, 77, 92);">Fractional Sales Leader</a> is responsible for a sales organization that runs without you in it. That's the deliverable. Everything on the list below is just that deliverable broken into parts.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">Why the system beats a super-closer</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Founders love the idea of hiring a closer, because closing is the part you can see. But the research points the other way. Harvard Business Review reported an 18% difference in revenue growth between companies with a formal sales process and companies without one. A super-closer takes their magic with them when they leave. A documented process is still there Monday morning. In a recurring-revenue business, where this year's sloppy deals become next year's churn, the process is worth more than any individual seller. Including you.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 3px solid rgb(242, 161, 0); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">If your revenue depends on one person's talent, you don't have a sales organization. You have a key-person risk with a CRM login.</blockquote><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-width: 1px medium medium; border-style: solid none none; border-color: rgb(226, 230, 237) currentcolor currentcolor; border-image: initial;"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">The Six Responsibilities That Belong in Writing</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">When I take on a B2B SaaS client, these six things go into the engagement in writing. If a fractional candidate won't commit to them on paper, keep interviewing.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">1. Sales process</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">One documented way to sell. Defined stages, exit criteria for each stage, and a qualification bar every rep applies the same way. Right now your process lives in your head, which is why nobody else can run it. Getting it out of your head and onto paper is job one.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">2. Hiring system</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Scorecards, structured interviews, and a ramp plan that exists before the offer letter does. Your Fractional Sales Leader should run the searches, the interviews, and the onboarding, so you stop betting $80K of salary and six months of runway on whoever tells the best stories in the interview.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">3. Coaching rhythm</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Weekly one-on-ones and call reviews that build skill instead of just collecting activity reports. This one has the best-documented payoff of the whole list: CSO Insights found teams with a formal coaching process post 28% higher win rates than teams that coach randomly or not at all. Random coaching is what founders do between board prep and product fires. That's not an insult. It's a schedule problem, and it's exactly what you're buying your way out of.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">4. Pipeline truth</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A forecast built on qualified deals instead of hopeful ones, with new pipeline creation inspected every week before anyone talks about closing. Founder-led pipelines are famously flattering. Somebody has to be responsible for making the number honest, and it can't be the person whose company valuation depends on it.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">5. Comp and quota math</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Compensation plans that pay for the behavior you actually want, and quotas reverse-engineered from your real win rates and deal sizes. The Bridge Group's 2026 report found just 48% of AEs hit annual quota. That's not a lazy-rep epidemic. That's an industry setting targets by wish. Your Fractional Sales Leader owns the math that keeps you out of that statistic.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">6. Accountability</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">An Accountabilities Document that names every number and the one person who owns it. Reps own their numbers. You own yours. And the Fractional Sales Leader's own deliverables sit in the same document, reviewed at the same weekly meeting. No hiding, in either direction.</p><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 0px; border-radius: 10px; overflow: hidden; box-shadow: rgba(14, 77, 92, 0.15) 0px 4px 20px;"><img src="https://ckffpnbnpvwmecwzovxf.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/media/articles/1783209292278_fsl_six_responsibilities.png" alt="Infographic titled The Six Things a Fractional Sales Leader Owns, showing six numbered cards on a dark teal background: 01 Sales Process, one documented way to sell with stages, exit criteria, and one qualification bar, tagged plus 18 percent revenue growth per HBR; 02 Hiring System, scorecards, structured interviews, and ramp plans; 03 Coaching Rhythm, weekly one-on-ones and call reviews, tagged plus 28 percent win rates per CSO Insights; 04 Pipeline Truth, a forecast built on qualified deals inspected weekly; 05 Comp and Quota, plans that pay for the behavior you want with quotas built from real win rates; 06 Accountability, an Accountabilities Document naming every number and its owner." style="width:100%;display:block;border-radius:10px;" /></div><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-width: 1px medium medium; border-style: solid none none; border-color: rgb(226, 230, 237) currentcolor currentcolor; border-image: initial;"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">What Are They Not Responsible For?</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">This half of the question matters just as much, because most fractional engagements don't fail on effort. They fail on scope. Here's where the line sits.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">They're not your quota-carrying rep</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A good Fractional Sales Leader will absolutely get on deals: joining calls, coaching live, helping structure the big ones. But if every closed deal has their fingerprints on it as the seller, you haven't left founder-led sales. You've outsourced it. The dependency just changed names, and it got more expensive. The job is to make your reps able to close, not to close instead of them.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">They're not your marketing department</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">They should give marketing a precise ICP, tight qualification feedback, and a clear definition of a sales-ready lead. What they shouldn't own is demand generation itself: ads, content, SEO, brand. When one fractional person owns both sales and marketing at your stage, both get half an owner, and you can't tell which half is failing when the pipeline goes quiet.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">They're not a rescue mission for product-market fit</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A sales system amplifies what's true. If customers buy, it helps them buy faster and more predictably. If customers fundamentally don't want the product, no sales leader on earth, fractional or full-time, fixes that. An honest one will show you the evidence in your win-loss data and say so to your face. That conversation is worth the whole engagement fee. But the fix belongs to you and your product team.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">And they're not permanent</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The engagement should have a built-in ending: a sales organization strong enough to justify a full-time leader, promoted from within or hired with the scorecards they left behind. A Fractional Sales Leader who needs to stay forever is telling you the system never got built.</p><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 0px; border-radius: 10px; overflow: hidden; box-shadow: rgba(14, 77, 92, 0.15) 0px 4px 20px;"><img src="https://ckffpnbnpvwmecwzovxf.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/media/articles/1783209293704_fsl_responsibility_line.png" alt="Two-column comparison infographic titled The Line of Responsibility on a dark teal background. Left column with green header labeled Responsible For lists: building the sales system and process, hiring, onboarding, and coaching reps, pipeline truth and an honest forecast, comp plans and quota math, and weekly accountability in writing. Right column with red header labeled Not Responsible For lists: carrying a quota as your only rep, owning marketing and demand gen, fixing product-market fit, closing every deal for you forever, and being a permanent executive salary line." style="width:100%;display:block;border-radius:10px;" /></div><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 3px solid rgb(242, 161, 0); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">The test is one question: if this person disappeared tomorrow, what's left? If the answer is "a process, a team, and a forecast I trust," you scoped it right.</blockquote><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-width: 1px medium medium; border-style: solid none none; border-color: rgb(226, 230, 237) currentcolor currentcolor; border-image: initial;"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">Why This Question Matters More in B2B SaaS</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The alternative to getting this scope right is the one you've been avoiding: hiring a full-time VP of Sales and hoping. The data on that bet is ugly. Jason Lemkin at SaaStr has said for years that around 70% of first-time VPs of Sales don't make it 12 months. Gong's analysis puts average VP of Sales tenure at about 19 months, down from 26. At a $250K-plus package, a miss doesn't just burn cash. It burns the year of pipeline that nobody was building while the wrong person reorganized your CRM.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">SaaS makes the miss even more expensive, because recurring revenue punishes bad selling twice. A mis-sold deal in a services business is one bad project. A mis-sold deal in SaaS is churn, a damaged logo, and a worse net revenue retention number when you raise. Between $1M and $10M ARR, you need senior sales leadership before you can justify the full-time price tag for it. That's precisely the gap fractional leadership exists to fill, and precisely why the responsibilities have to be written down. You're buying a fraction of someone's time. You can't afford a fraction of their accountability.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-width: 1px medium medium; border-style: solid none none; border-color: rgb(226, 230, 237) currentcolor currentcolor; border-image: initial;"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">How to Hold a Fractional Sales Leader Accountable</h2><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">Put it in an Accountabilities Document</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Before the engagement starts, both of you sign an Accountabilities Document. Theirs should name the system deliverables: documented process by day 30, qualification bar and pipeline scrub by day 45, hiring scorecards and comp math by day 60, coaching rhythm running by day 90. Dates, not vibes.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">Then inspect the numbers weekly</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">After the first 90 days, you judge them on the system's output: qualified pipeline created per week, win rate trend, rep ramp time, and forecast accuracy. Those four numbers tell you whether the machine is getting built or whether you're paying for expensive company. And notice what's not on that list: their personal closed-won. The moment you grade a Fractional Sales Leader like a rep, they'll act like a rep, and you'll be right back where you started. The founder who does everything, plus an invoice.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 3px solid rgb(242, 161, 0); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">Scope the role around the system and you'll know within 90 days if it's working. Scope it around heroics and you'll find out in a year, the expensive way.</blockquote><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-width: 1px medium medium; border-style: solid none none; border-color: rgb(226, 230, 237) currentcolor currentcolor; border-image: initial;"><a href="https://louiebernstein.com/articles/questions-to-ask-fractional-sales-leader" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 20px 24px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(14, 77, 92); border-radius: 0px 8px 8px 0px; display: flex; align-items: center; gap: 12px; text-decoration: none; background: rgba(14, 77, 92, 0.05);"><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;"><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 4px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700; letter-spacing: 0.1em; text-transform: uppercase; display: block; color: rgb(14, 77, 92);">Related Reading</span><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.35;">Questions to Ask a Fractional Sales Leader Before You Hire One →</span></div></a><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">Frequently Asked Questions</h2><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: What is a fractional sales leader responsible for?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">A Fractional Sales Leader is responsible for building and running your sales system: a documented sales process with stages and exit criteria, a hiring system with scorecards and ramp plans, a weekly coaching rhythm, an honest pipeline and forecast, compensation and quota math built from real win rates, and written accountability for every number. The deliverable is a sales organization that produces revenue without depending on the founder.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: What is a fractional sales leader not responsible for?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">They're not responsible for carrying a quota as your primary rep, owning marketing and demand generation, fixing product-market fit, or staying forever. They'll join deals to coach and help structure them, and they'll feed marketing a sharp ICP and lead definition. But if the engagement is scoped around them personally closing your deals, you've replaced founder-led sales with rented-closer sales, and the dependency problem survives.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: Should a fractional sales leader carry a quota?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">No, not a personal one. Grade them on the system's output instead: qualified pipeline created per week, win rate trend, rep ramp time, and forecast accuracy. A personal quota pushes them to sell instead of build, which feels great for a quarter and leaves you with nothing when they roll off. The team's revenue target is the scoreboard; their job is making the team able to hit it.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How many hours a week does a fractional sales leader work?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Most engagements run 10 to 15 hours a week, and the range across the industry is roughly 5 to 25. But hours are the wrong unit to buy. Scope the engagement around deliverables with dates: process documented by day 30, hiring system by day 60, coaching rhythm live by day 90. A leader who hits those in 12 hours a week is worth more than one who logs 25 and delivers slideware.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How is a fractional sales leader different from a sales consultant?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">A consultant hands you a recommendation and leaves the execution to you. A Fractional Sales Leader executes: they run the pipeline meetings, do the hiring, coach the reps, and own numbers inside your business, on your team, every week. If the engagement ends with a deck instead of a working sales system and a trained team, you bought consulting with a fancier title.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: When should a B2B SaaS founder hire a fractional sales leader instead of a VP of Sales?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Between roughly $1M and $10M ARR, when sales still runs through the founder and the budget can't absorb a $250K-plus VP of Sales mistake.
