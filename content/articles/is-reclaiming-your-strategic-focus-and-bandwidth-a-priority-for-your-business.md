Work nobody should be doing</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Reports nobody reads. Standing meetings that no longer have a purpose. Approvals that no longer add value. They survive because they were useful at $1M and nobody has had time to retire them at $5M.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Run an honest pass through two weeks of calendar. You hire the help and keep doing the work. You sit in deal reviews. You email reps directly. Every time you do that, you tell the team the new leader is decorative.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Decide what counts as escalation and what does not. You still need to see what is happening. The fix is reporting, not presence. A weekly pipeline view, accurate forecasting, real numbers on close rates and stage conversion. If it isn't in the CRM, it never happened. That discipline lets you stop attending every meeting without losing the picture.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">Quitting after one bad week</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The first ninety days of a hand-off always include at least one ugly week. A deal stalls. The new leader makes a call you would not have made. Most founders panic and pull the work back. Do not do that. Pull the work back and you reset the clock to zero. Sit through the ugly week. Coach the leader. Then keep going.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 3px solid rgb(232, 97, 10); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">The point of stepping back is not to stop caring. It is to stop being the only one who can. Those are different things, and the team will feel the difference.</blockquote><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-width: 1px medium medium; border-style: solid none none; border-color: rgb(226, 230, 237) currentcolor currentcolor; border-image: initial;"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">Frequently Asked Questions</h2><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How do I know if my bandwidth problem is real?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Run the calendar audit honestly. If less than ten percent of your week is in the strategy bucket, the problem is real. If you cannot remember the last full afternoon you spent thinking about the business instead of running it, the problem is real. If your leadership team waits on you for decisions that should not need you, the problem is real. None of that is complaining. It is data.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: I tried to step out of sales before and the numbers dropped. Why would this be different?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Because last time you stepped out without building anything where the founder used to be. Then step out.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: Is a Fractional Sales Leader really enough, or do I need a full-time VP of Sales?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">At $1M to $10M ARR, the answer is almost always Fractional. A full-time VP at this stage runs $250,000 to $400,000 all in, with equity on top, and the risk of a bad hire is enormous. A Fractional Sales Leader does the same work, sets up the same system, for a fraction of the cost. Past $10M ARR, when the pipeline supports a full-time leader, the math changes. Before that, full-time is usually an expensive mistake.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How long does it take to feel the bandwidth come back?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">The first noticeable change usually hits around week six. The first full month is heavy because you are still in everything as the hand-off happens. By week six, the new leader owns the pipeline and the team, the reporting is in place, and you stop being copied on every email. By month three, the calendar looks different. By month six, the strategy work that has been pushed for two years actually starts to happen.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: What if I enjoy selling and do not want to step away from it entirely?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Good. You should not. Stay close to the top three to five customer relationships and the strategic deals. That is real CEO selling, and your involvement moves the needle. Step away from the operational sales work: pipeline reviews, mid-market deal management, rep coaching, onboarding. That is what eats the calendar. Reserve your sales time for the deals that need the CEO in the room.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How do I make the case to my co-founder or board that this is the right move?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Bring the math.
