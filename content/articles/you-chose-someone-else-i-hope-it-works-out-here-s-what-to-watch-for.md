I do not know who you chose or why. What I do know is the patterns that lead founders to make suboptimal decisions when evaluating fractional sales help. I know, because I have been on the other side of these conversations for years.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 12px; padding: 0px;">The resume that looks right but is built for a different stage</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px;">Many fractional sales leaders have genuinely impressive backgrounds. They have been VPs of Sales at companies you have heard of. They have managed large teams, exceeded big quotas, and have the LinkedIn profile to prove it.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px;">The problem is that most of that experience was accumulated at companies where the infrastructure already existed. The CRM was already configured. The playbook was already written. The reps were already trained. Their job was to optimize a machine someone else had built.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px;">At the $1M–$10M ARR stage, you do not need someone to optimize a machine. You need someone to build one from scratch. Those are different skills. A strong record of optimization does not prove the ability to build.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 12px; padding: 0px;">The consultant who arrived with a framework</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px;">Some people come into these engagements with a methodology they have used at previous clients. It has a name. It has a slide deck. It looks professional and systematic.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px;">The question is whether the methodology was designed for your specific situation — your stage, your industry, your team, your founder — or whether it is a template being applied to another company.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px;">Good fractional sales leadership is custom work. It extracts your institutional knowledge, your customer insights, and your sales experience, and turns them into a documented system that is specific to your business. A pre-packaged framework applied generically will produce generic results.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 12px; padding: 0px;">The choice that was really about comfort</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px;">Sometimes founders choose a particular person not because of evidence they can build what is needed, but because they felt more comfortable. Same industry. Similar background. The conversations felt easier.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px;">Comfort is not a bad signal — the relationship matters. But comfort in conversation and competence in execution are not the same thing. The most important question is never "did I enjoy talking to this person?" It is "can this person build what I actually need?"</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 232, 240);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 16px; padding: 0px; letter-spacing: -0.015em;">Five Things to Watch for in the First 90 Days</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px;">Regardless of who you are working with, these are the signals that tell you whether the engagement is on track — or heading toward the result you have seen before.</p><ul style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 0px; margin-bottom: 20px; margin-left: 0px; padding: 0px 0px 0px 24px; list-style: none; display: flex; flex-direction: column; gap: 8px;"><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65; display: flex; align-items: flex-start; gap: 10px;"><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 3px 0px 0px; padding: 0px; flex-shrink: 0;">✅</span><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 600;">By Day 30, a Version 1 Sales Playbook exists.</span>Not a plan to write one. Not an outline. An actual document with your ICP defined, your discovery questions written out, your objection responses documented. If a month has passed and there is still nothing written down, the engagement is already behind.</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65; display: flex; align-items: flex-start; gap: 10px;"><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 3px 0px 0px; padding: 0px; flex-shrink: 0;">✅</span><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 600;">By Day 30, your CRM reflects reality.</span>The pipeline stages match your actual sales process. Opportunities are moving through stages based on actions the prospect has taken — not what the rep thinks will happen. If your CRM is still a wishful thinking exercise at 30 days, nothing fundamental has changed yet.</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65; display: flex; align-items: flex-start; gap: 10px;"><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 3px 0px 0px; padding: 0px; flex-shrink: 0;">✅</span><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 600;">By Day 45, a coaching cadence is running.</span>Weekly pipeline reviews. Daily or weekly rep standups. Specific, documented feedback on calls. If your reps are not being actively coached on a defined schedule, the engagement is advisory — not operational.</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65; display: flex; align-items: flex-start; gap: 10px;"><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 3px 0px 0px; padding: 0px; flex-shrink: 0;">✅</span><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 600;">By Day 60, you are spending less time on sales, not the same amount.</span>If you are still the person closing most deals and your fractional leader is "learning the business," that is not an engagement — that is an extended onboarding at your expense. The work should be reducing your involvement, not requiring it.</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65; display: flex; align-items: flex-start; gap: 10px;"><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 3px 0px 0px; padding: 0px; flex-shrink: 0;">✅</span><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 600;">By Day 90, your reps are executing a process — not winging it.</span>They have a playbook they follow. If your team is still operating the same way they were before the engagement started, nothing has been built yet.</li></ul><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 20px; border-left-width: 4px; border-left-color: rgb(232, 97, 10); border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 20px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.6; margin-top: 0px !important; margin-right: 0px !important; margin-left: 0px !important;">An engagement that produces a binder, a deck, or a set of recommendations is a consulting engagement. Have it now.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px;">Ask your fractional leader directly: "Where is the playbook? Why is my pipeline still inaccurate? If their answer describes optimizing or scaling something that already existed — they are an optimizer. If their answer describes writing the first playbook, configuring the CRM from zero, and hiring the first reps — they are a builder. Your stage at $1M–$10M ARR almost certainly requires a builder.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px;"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: What questions should I ask my fractional leader in the first 30 days?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.7;">Three questions that tell you everything: "Where is the Version 1 Sales Playbook?" "Why does this opportunity in the CRM show as Stage 3 — what action did the prospect take to earn that stage?" "What specific change in my reps' behavior have you seen in the last two weeks?" Vague answers to all three are a signal. Specific, concrete answers mean the work is actually happening.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px;"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: Is it too late to switch if the engagement is not working?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.7;">Never. The cost of continuing a non-productive engagement is always higher than the cost of ending it. If you have documentation from the current engagement — even incomplete — that is a starting point for the next one. If you do not have documentation, that tells you something important about whether the work was being done. Either way, the answer is always to stop compounding a bad investment and make a better one.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px;"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: If I come back to you after a failed engagement, where do we start?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.7;">We start with what exists. Sometimes a previous engagement has produced partial infrastructure — a rough playbook, some CRM setup, a half-built process. We take what is useful, fix what is wrong, and build what is missing. The audit I run at the beginning of every engagement identifies exactly what state the sales system is in. We build from there.
