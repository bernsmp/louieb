I'll give you the case for a fractional SDR and the case against. Then I'll tell you what I'd actually do if I were sitting in your chair at $1M–$10M ARR.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">First, What a Fractional SDR Actually Is</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A Sales Development Rep, sometimes called a BDR, is the person at the top of the funnel. They don't close. Their whole job is to start conversations. Cold email, cold calls, LinkedIn outreach, following up on the leads your marketing brings in. The goal is one thing: booked meetings for whoever closes, which right now is probably you.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A <em style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">fractional</em> SDR is that same role, part-time. Usually through an agency or a contractor, usually somewhere between a few hundred and a few thousand dollars a month depending on how many hours and how much they're doing for you. The pitch is seductive. You get pipeline without a full salary, without benefits, without a long-term commitment.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">That pitch is half true. And the half that isn't true is the half that costs you.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">The Case FOR a Fractional SDR</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">I'm not here to trash outbound. I built a company on it. So let me make the honest argument for why a fractional SDR can be a smart move.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">You get pipeline fast, and you get it on demand</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Inbound takes months to build. Outbound can put a meeting on your calendar this week. If you have a short runway, a new product to validate, or a specific list of dream accounts you want in front of, outbound is the only channel that lets you go get them on purpose instead of waiting for them to find you.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">It's a fraction of the cost of a full-time hire</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A full-time in-house SDR is not cheap once you load in everything. Look at the real number before you assume part-time is the obvious answer.</p><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 0px; border-radius: 10px; overflow: hidden; box-shadow: rgba(58, 42, 82, 0.15) 0px 4px 20px;"><img src="https://ckffpnbnpvwmecwzovxf.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/media/articles/1784762917923_sdr-cost.png" alt="Infographic: the fully loaded annual cost of one in-house SDR, broken into base salary, variable comp, benefits and payroll tax, tools and data, manager overhead, recruiting and onboarding, and ramp productivity loss, totaling $98K to $173K per year" style="width:100%;display:block;border-radius:10px;" /></div><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A fully loaded in-house SDR runs $98,000 to $173,000 a year once you add benefits, tools, management, recruiting, and ramp loss (SalesHive, Martal). Against that, a fractional SDR at $2,000–$4,000 a month looks like a bargain. If you genuinely can't justify a full seat yet, part-time lets you test the channel without betting the year on it.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">A good agency comes with tooling and reps who've done it before</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The better outbound agencies bring the data, the sequencing software, the deliverability setup, and reps who've run thousands of calls. You're not building an outbound stack from scratch. For a founder who has never run outbound, that shortcut has real value, as long as you remember it's a shortcut, not a system.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left-color: rgb(242, 103, 78); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">Outbound isn't dead. It's just unforgiving. It rewards precision and punishes everything else.</blockquote><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">The Case AGAINST a Fractional SDR</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Now the other side. And this is where I land for most of the founders I work with.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">The real cost isn't their fee. It's your time</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The /ceo program I run values a founder's time at $300 an hour. Now do the math on an SDR. To get a part-time rep producing, you're looking at product training, ICP training, script writing, objection handling, and then daily call reviews and email reviews to keep them on track. That's not a couple hours a month. Done right, outbound coaching is a 15-minute session <em style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">every single morning</em>, listening to the good calls, reinforcing what works. I've taught sales managers to do exactly that. It's the right way to run a rep. But you're not a sales manager. You're the founder, and every hour you spend babysitting outbound is an hour you're not closing deals or building the business.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Outbound results are thin right now, and a part-timer rarely beats the average</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Let's be honest about the numbers. Cold email reply rates sit around 1–5% for most campaigns (Belkins, QuickMail). Cold call connect rates run just 4–9% of dials reaching a live human (Cognism), and the average B2B SDR books roughly one qualified meeting every 40 to 80 dials. Cold outreach closes at about 1.7% (HubSpot). Those are the averages for full-time reps with support. A part-time rep who isn't dialed into your business, working your list a few hours a week, tends to land <em style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">below</em> average, not above it.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">The revolving door eats your investment</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Even full-time SDRs churn hard. Average tenure is about 14–18 months and annual turnover runs 30–39% (Orum, Bridge Group). Ramp time to full productivity is roughly 3.2 months. So you invest months getting someone competent, and the clock is already running out. With a fractional contractor rotating across other clients, the loyalty and continuity are even shakier. You can retrain that person every quarter and never build any compounding value.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">The same money often buys more in inbound</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">This is the argument I keep coming back to for founders at your stage. Inbound leads cost roughly 60% less per lead than outbound, and they convert far better because the buyer came to you with intent already in hand.</p><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 0px; border-radius: 10px; overflow: hidden; box-shadow: rgba(58, 42, 82, 0.15) 0px 4px 20px;"><img src="https://ckffpnbnpvwmecwzovxf.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/media/articles/1784762925978_sdr-vs-inbound.png" alt="Comparison infographic: fractional SDR versus shifting budget to inbound, comparing cost per lead ($300-$500 outbound vs ~60% cheaper inbound), close rate (1.7% cold outreach vs 14.6% SEO leads), founder time to train and track (heavy vs light), and speed to first meeting (days to weeks outbound vs 3-6 months to mature inbound)" style="width:100%;display:block;border-radius:10px;" /></div><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Look at the close rates. For a founder with limited hours and limited dollars, shifting some of that outbound budget into content, LinkedIn, and a search-friendly site can produce more qualified conversations with less of your time. It's slower to start. It compounds. And unlike a contractor, the assets you build stay yours forever.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left-color: rgb(242, 103, 78); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">A fractional SDR with no system is just a more expensive way to be disappointed. Fix the system first, then decide who runs the channel.</blockquote><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">The Real Question: Do You Have a System, or Just a Seat?</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Here's what fifty years taught me. The founders who hire an SDR and get burned aren't victims of outbound. They're victims of hiring a person to fill a hole where a system should be.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Outbound works, when it works, because of what's around the rep. A tight ICP so you're not spraying strangers. A real script that's been tested, not improvised. Clean data. Four funnel stages at most, so you can actually forecast. A daily coaching cadence. And KPIs that measure the leading activity, not just the lagging result. When those things exist, an SDR, fractional or full-time, has a fighting chance. When they don't, you're paying someone to fail slowly.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">So before you spend a dollar on a fractional SDR, answer these:</p><ul style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 0px; margin-bottom: 22px; margin-left: 0px; padding: 0px; list-style: none;"><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 8px 0px 8px 32px; position: relative; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237); line-height: 1.55;">Can you name your ICP in one sentence, with the specific trigger that makes them a buyer right now?</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 8px 0px 8px 32px; position: relative; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237); line-height: 1.55;">Do you have a written script and sequence that's already converted, or would the rep be guessing?</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 8px 0px 8px 32px; position: relative; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237); line-height: 1.55;">Who is going to coach and track this person every week, and is that realistically going to be you?</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 8px 0px 8px 32px; position: relative; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237); line-height: 1.55;">Do you have an Accountabilities Document that spells out exactly what "good" looks like for the role?</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 8px 0px 8px 32px; position: relative; border-bottom: none; line-height: 1.55;">Would that same money produce more qualified conversations in inbound over the next two quarters?</li></ul><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">If you can't answer those cleanly, you don't have an SDR problem. You have a system problem. And no hire, fractional or full-time, fixes a system problem. It just puts a face on it.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">This is exactly the work I do with founders in the <a href="https://louiebernstein.com/ceo" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 600; text-decoration-line: underline; text-underline-offset: 2px;">Founder's Corner</a>. My Honest Answer</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Yes, in exactly one situation. You have a proven, written outbound motion that's already produced meetings, a tight ICP, and someone other than you to coach and track the rep. In that case a fractional SDR is a smart, capital-efficient way to scale a channel you've already validated.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">No, in the situation most $1M–$10M founders are actually in. You're still closing most deals yourself, you don't have a documented outbound system, and the SDR would report to a founder who has no time to manage them. In that case, the fee is the smallest part of what it'll cost you, and your dollars will almost always do more shifted into inbound while you build the system underneath.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The channel isn't your problem. The missing system is. Fix that, and the SDR question answers itself.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><a href="https://louiebernstein.com/articles/why-do-my-sales-reps-keep-getting-ghosted-by-their-prospects" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 20px 24px; border-radius: 8px; display: flex; align-items: center; gap: 12px; text-decoration-line: none;"><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;"><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 4px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700; letter-spacing: 0.1em; text-transform: uppercase; display: block;">Related Reading</span><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.35;">Why Do My Sales Reps Keep Getting Ghosted by Their Prospects? →</span></div></a><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">Frequently Asked Questions</h2><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: What's the difference between a fractional SDR and a fractional BDR?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">In practice, very little. Both titles describe the top-of-funnel role that starts conversations and books meetings but doesn't close. Some companies use SDR for reps who work inbound leads and BDR for reps who do pure cold outbound, but the terms are used interchangeably far more often than not. "Fractional" just means part-time, usually through an agency or contractor. Whichever label you use, the economics and the management burden I describe above are the same.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How much does a fractional SDR cost?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Most fractional or outsourced SDR arrangements run $2,000 to $6,000 a month depending on hours, activity volume, and whether the agency supplies data and tooling. That's genuinely cheaper than a fully loaded in-house SDR, which runs $98,000 to $173,000 a year. But the fee is only the visible cost. The hidden cost is the founder time required to train, coach, and track the rep, plus the low return if there's no system around them. Cheap and effective are not the same thing.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: Is outbound sales still worth it in 2026?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Outbound still works, but it's less forgiving than it used to be. Cold email reply rates sit around 1–5% and cold call connect rates around 4–9% of dials. The teams that win with outbound have a tight ICP, tested scripts, clean data, and daily coaching. The teams that lose treat it as a spray-and-pray activity. So the honest answer is: outbound is worth it if you run it as a disciplined system, and a waste of money if you don't. That's true whether the rep is full-time, fractional, or you.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: Should I invest in inbound marketing instead of a fractional SDR?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">For a lot of $1M–$10M founders, yes, at least with part of the budget. Inbound leads cost about 60% less per lead and close at dramatically higher rates because the buyer arrives with intent. The tradeoff is time: inbound takes three to six months to mature, where outbound can book a meeting this week. The smartest founders don't pick one forever. They use inbound to build a compounding, lower-cost engine and use targeted outbound to go after specific dream accounts. What they don't do is hand outbound to an unmanaged part-timer and call it a strategy.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How much of my time will a fractional SDR really take?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">More than the sales pitch admits. To run an SDR properly you want a short coaching session most mornings, listening to their best calls and reinforcing what works, plus weekly pipeline and KPI review. If you're the one doing that, budget several hours a week, every week, indefinitely. That's the number founders forget to put on the invoice. If you don't have those hours, either don't hire the SDR yet or bring in someone whose job is to manage the channel for you.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: When does a fractional SDR actually make sense?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">When you've already proven the outbound motion yourself, you can hand the rep a written ICP, a tested script, and a clear Accountabilities Document, and you have someone other than the founder to coach and track them. In that situation a fractional SDR is a capital-efficient way to scale a channel you know converts. The mistake is using a fractional SDR to <em>discover</em> whether outbound works for you.
