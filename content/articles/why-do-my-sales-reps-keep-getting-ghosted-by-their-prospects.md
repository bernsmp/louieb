Twenty-three deals sitting in "Proposal Sent." Looked great on the dashboard.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">I asked him one question. "How many of those have a next meeting on the calendar?" He checked. Two.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The other twenty-one weren't deals. They were ghosts. Prospects who took the call, said "this looks interesting," and then vanished. No reply to the follow-up. No reply to the follow-up to the follow-up.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><b>So he asked me the question I hear from founders every month: "Why do my sales reps keep getting ghosted?"</b> Here's the answer, and here's four ways to fix it.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">Why Prospects Ghost Your Reps in the First Place</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Sales reps get ghosted because the prospect never felt enough value to keep talking, never agreed to a concrete next step, and the follow-ups gave them nothing new to respond to. Ghosting isn't rudeness. It's a verdict. The prospect graded the conversation, and they didn't mail the report card.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The research backs this up. Matt Dixon's team analyzed more than 2.5 million recorded sales conversations for the book The JOLT Effect and found that 40 to 60 percent of B2B deals are lost to no decision. Not to a competitor. To nothing. And prospects almost never call to tell you they've decided to do nothing. Saying no feels confrontational. Disappearing feels easy. So they disappear.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Here's the part founders don't want to hear. You probably don't get ghosted much yourself. You're the founder. You carry authority, product passion, and the ability to make a deal on the spot. Your reps don't inherit any of that. They inherit your sales process. And if you never built one, they inherited nothing. They're winging discovery, winging follow-up, and getting graded on it by every prospect they talk to.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(242, 161, 0); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">"A prospect who ghosts your rep already gave you their answer. They just didn't say it out loud."</blockquote><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">Why Does a Prospect Ghost a Sales Rep on the Initial Discovery Call?</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Sometimes the ghost shows up before the relationship even starts. The prospect books the discovery call and never shows, or takes the call and vanishes that same week. When that happens, one of three things went wrong: the prospect said yes to be polite, the gap between booking and the call killed their urgency, or they expected a pitch dressed up as a conversation. All three have a fix, and all three fixes belong to the rep, not the prospect.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">They said yes to get off the phone</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A meeting booked off a weak conversation produces a no-show. If your rep pushed for the calendar before the prospect ever named a real problem, the prospect agreed because agreeing was easier than declining. They never intended to show. The fix is to qualify before you book. Have the rep ask, "What would make this call worth twenty minutes of your time?" If the prospect can't name the problem the meeting is supposed to solve, your rep doesn't have a meeting. They have a courtesy. Don't book courtesies.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">The booking-to-call gap went silent</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A discovery call booked three weeks out with nothing in between is an invitation to be forgotten. Keep the gap short. Five business days or less whenever possible. Then fill it. Within an hour of booking, the rep sends an agenda that tells the prospect exactly what they'll walk away with, even if they never buy a thing. The day before, the rep confirms with one useful insight attached. Not "looking forward to connecting." Something the prospect would want to read anyway. Every touch between booking and call is one more reason to show up.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">They expected an ambush</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Your prospects have been burned. They've booked "discovery calls" that turned out to be 45-minute feature tours with a contract waiting at the end. So they protect themselves the easy way. They don't show. The fix goes right in the calendar invite: "No demo. No pitch. Twenty minutes on [their specific problem], and if I don't think we can help, I'll tell you that too." Remove the threat and you remove the reason to hide.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">And when a no-show still happens? No guilt trips. The rep reschedules once, with something of value attached. If the prospect no-shows twice, close the file with a polite two-line note and move on. Two no-shows isn't bad luck. It's an answer.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(242, 161, 0); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">"A no-show isn't a calendar problem. It's a value problem. The prospect did the math, and your meeting lost."</blockquote><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">Here's Four Ways to Fix This</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Ghosting gets fixed in four places: before the call, during the call, at the end of the call, and after the call. Make your reps do their homework before every call</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Your prospect takes five sales calls a week. Four of them open with a generic pitch the rep could've given to anybody. Generic openers earn generic silence. Here's the fix I give every team I work with: before any call, run the prospect's website through AI. List the top five features and their benefits. List the top competitors. Pull the pricing if it's public. Pull anything significant from the About page. Five minutes of prep, and your rep walks into the call sounding like a Trusted Advisor instead of pitch number four of the prospect's week. Prospects don't ghost people who clearly did the work. It feels rude. That works in your favor.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Fix 2. Fix the discovery call, because that's where ghosting starts</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Most ghosting doesn't happen in the follow-up. It happens three weeks earlier, on a discovery call where the rep pitched features instead of asking about pain. The framework I teach is simple. Open with the one problem keeping that prospect up at night, and get specific. Tell a quick story about a customer just like them who solved it. Then ask the How question: "How are you dealing with this today?" Then shut up and listen. No pitch. No appointment grab. Let the questions qualify the prospect in or out. A prospect who spent the call talking about their own problem doesn't ghost the person who listened. And a prospect with no real pain and no timeline was never going to buy anyway. Better your rep learns that on call one than after six unanswered emails.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Fix 3. Never end a call without a calendared next step</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">"Sounds great, I'll follow up next week" is the sentence that kills more deals than any competitor. The next step gets booked while everyone's still on the call. Specific date. Specific time. Specific agenda. Calendar invite sent and accepted before anyone hangs up. If a prospect won't commit to twenty minutes two weeks out, that's not a scheduling problem. That's data. They're telling your rep the interest isn't there, and they're telling them for free. Make this a rule on your team: a deal without a calendared next step isn't a deal. It's a hope.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Fix 4. Replace "just checking in" with something worth replying to</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">"Just checking in" and "bumping this to the top of your inbox" are asks. They ask the prospect to do your rep's job: re-read the thread, remember why they cared, and find time they don't have. Nobody replies to homework. Every follow-up has to give something instead. A case study from a company in their industry. An answer to a question they raised on the last call. A relevant insight about their market. One new reason to keep the conversation alive. If your rep has nothing left to give, the follow-up sequence is over, and that's the honest signal to send the breakup email and move on.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(242, 161, 0); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">"'Just checking in' is a withdrawal. Your reps need to make deposits."</blockquote><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">The Difference Between Reps Who Get Ghosted and Reps Who Don't</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">After fifty years in sales, I can tell you ghosting isn't evenly distributed. No tricks. No "open loops." No guilt-trip emails asking if the prospect "fell off a cliff." Those tactics get opens. They don't get deals. The right column is just disciplined selling, and discipline is teachable.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">If Every Rep Is Getting Ghosted, It's Not the Reps</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Here's the test I give founders. If one rep is getting ghosted and the others aren't, you have a coaching problem. Coach the rep. But if your whole team is getting ghosted, you have a process problem, and process problems belong to the founder.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Ask yourself what your reps were actually given. Is there a documented discovery framework, or did they learn by shadowing you twice? Is there a follow-up cadence with pre-built, value-first touches, or does each rep improvise? Is there a weekly pipeline review where "proposal sent, no next step on the calendar" gets caught and fixed, or does the dashboard just quietly fill with ghosts? At $1M to $10M ARR, the honest answer is usually none of the above. Not because you don't care. And when you're eventually ready for that full-time hire, they walk into a system that already works.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(242, 161, 0); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">"Your reps don't inherit your authority or your passion. If you never built one, they inherited nothing."</blockquote><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><a href="https://louiebernstein.com/articles/how-to-run-a-discovery-call-with-a-new-prospect" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 20px 24px; border-radius: 8px; display: flex; align-items: center; gap: 12px; text-decoration-line: none; background: rgb(244, 246, 250); border-left: 4px solid rgb(14, 77, 92);"><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;"><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 4px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700; letter-spacing: 0.1em; text-transform: uppercase; display: block; color: rgb(14, 77, 92);">Related Reading</span><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.35; color: rgb(14, 77, 92);">How to Run a Discovery Call With a New Prospect →</span></div></a><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">Frequently Asked Questions</h2><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: Why do prospects ghost sales reps?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Prospects ghost sales reps because they never felt enough value to keep the conversation going, there was no concrete next step on the calendar, and the follow-ups asked for time without offering anything new. Saying no feels confrontational, so prospects take the easier path and disappear. Research on more than 2.5 million sales calls found that 40 to 60 percent of B2B deals end in no decision, and ghosting is usually how a no-decision loss looks from the rep's side of the inbox.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: Why does a prospect ghost a sales rep on the initial discovery call?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Three reasons cover almost every case. The prospect agreed to the meeting to be polite and never intended to show. The gap between booking and the call went silent, so the urgency they felt when they said yes faded before the meeting arrived. Or they expected an ambush, a pitch disguised as a conversation, and protected themselves by not showing up. The rep fixes all three: qualify before booking, keep the gap under five business days and fill it with an agenda and a useful insight, and state "no demo, no pitch" right in the calendar invite. If a prospect no-shows twice, close the file. That's an answer, not bad luck.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How do I re-engage a prospect who ghosted my rep?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Lead with new value, not guilt. Send something the prospect would want even if they never buy: a case study from their industry, a relevant insight, or an answer to a question they raised on the last call. Switch channels too. If email is going unanswered, try a short phone call or a LinkedIn message. And give them an easy out: "If priorities changed, tell me and I'll close the file." That sentence gets more replies than ten bump emails, because it removes the awkwardness that caused the ghosting in the first place.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How many follow-ups should a rep send before moving on?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Five to seven value-adding touches over four to six weeks, spread across email, phone, and LinkedIn, is plenty for most B2B deals. But the real rule isn't a number. It's this: when your rep has nothing new to give, the sequence is over. Repeating "just checking in" past that point doesn't revive deals. It trains the prospect to ignore your company. End with a polite breakup email and put the energy into prospects who are actually moving.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: Should my reps send a breakup email?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Yes. A breakup email is short, polite, and gives the prospect a graceful exit: "I haven't heard back, so I'll assume this isn't a priority right now and close the file. If that changes, you know where to find me." It works for two reasons. It often gets a reply, because people respond to closure faster than they respond to pressure. And when it doesn't get a reply, it cleans the ghost out of your pipeline so your forecast stops lying to you. Either outcome beats a deal rotting in "Proposal Sent" for six months.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: Is ghosting my reps' fault or my sales process's fault?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Run the simple test. If one rep gets ghosted at a much higher rate than the rest of the team, that's a skill gap, and coaching fixes it. If every rep is getting ghosted, the process is the problem: no discovery framework, no qualification criteria, no follow-up cadence, no pipeline review catching dead deals early. At $1M to $10M ARR, it's almost always the process, because the founder has been too busy selling to document how they sell. Your reps can't copy a system that only exists in your head.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How does a Fractional Sales Leader reduce ghosting?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">A Fractional Sales Leader fixes the four places ghosting happens. They build the pre-call research routine, install a pain-first discovery framework, make "calendared next step" a non-negotiable rule, and write the value-first follow-up cadence every rep runs. Then they enforce it in a weekly pipeline review where deals without next steps get flagged immediately. You get all of that two to three days a week for $7K to $15K a month, instead of paying $250K+ for a full-time VP of Sales your company isn't ready for yet.</p></div><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 48px 0px 32px; padding: 40px 36px; border-radius: 10px; text-align: center; background: rgb(244, 246, 250); border: 1px solid rgb(14, 77, 92);"><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 12px; padding: 0px; color: rgb(14, 77, 92);">How many of your "deals" have a next meeting on the calendar?</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 24px; padding: 0px; color: rgb(77, 107, 114);">If you don't know, that's the problem.
