It's leadership.</text></svg></div><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Sign 1. Growth has flatlined</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Your revenue chart looks more like a plateau than a mountain. Four quarters in the same range. What got you to $3M won't get you to $5M. That ceiling is almost always you. Not because you're not good. Because you're one person, and one person doesn't scale.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Sign 2. You're leading a department you don't understand</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">You're a product genius. Or an operations wizard. Or a brilliant engineer. But you're faking it when it comes to running pipeline meetings, coaching salespeople, and building a sales process. You can sell because you know the product cold. You can't manage sellers because you've never built a system for someone else to sell inside.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Sign 3. Your day is 100% reactive</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Your calendar is filled with fires, not strategy. You haven't had a deep-work day in months. Every "important" meeting is somebody else's emergency. That isn't a workload problem. That's a structural problem. And no founder ever fixed it by working harder.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Sign 4. You need a sales leader but can't afford the salary</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">You know you need senior-level strategic thinking, but a $250,000+ salary plus benefits, equity, and a six-figure ramp isn't in the budget. This is the most common sign at $1M to $10M ARR. The need is real. The salary isn't realistic. That's exactly the situation a Fractional Sales Leader was built for.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Sign 5. Your team is all tactics, no strategy</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Everyone is busy. Calls are dialed. Emails are sent. Activity is high. Revenue is flat. There's no unifying process, no Sales Playbook, no shared definition of what good looks like. Activity without strategy is the most expensive kind of chaos because it feels productive.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Sign 6. You're preparing for a transition</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Fundraising, merging, or planning an exit. Buyers and investors don't pay a premium for a sales engine that runs on the founder. They pay a premium for one that runs on a system. If a transition is on the horizon, building the sales leadership layer before you go to market is the difference between a 4X and a 7X multiple.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(242, 161, 0); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">"Firing yourself from a role you're not meant to do isn't a sign of failure. It's a sign of leadership. It's the strategic move that unlocks the next level of growth for you and your company."</blockquote><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">The Three Real Options. Not One.</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Most founders think their only option is a full-time VP of Sales. That's the option LinkedIn and your investors will push you toward. It's also the most expensive and the most likely to fail at $1M to $10M ARR.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">There are three real options. Each one fits a different stage. Picking the wrong one is the $250,000 mistake.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Option 1. A Sales Manager</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Best for: You have 2 to 5 reps, a working playbook, and you need somebody to run the weekly cadence, coach the reps, and own the pipeline reviews. Not strategy. Execution. Cost is usually $90K to $140K base plus commission.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A Sales Manager isn't building the system. They're running the one you've built. If you don't have a Sales Playbook in place, a Sales Manager will fail the same way a VP would. They have no map either. They just have a smaller paycheck.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Option 2. A Fractional Sales Leader</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Best for: You're between $1M and $10M ARR. You don't have a Sales Playbook yet, or you have a draft that nobody's actually using. You need senior-level strategic thinking and execution two to three days a week, not forty hours. You can't afford a full-time VP and you shouldn't be hiring one anyway because the foundation isn't built.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A Fractional Sales Leader shows up with proven systems, not a request for yours. They roll up their sleeves and build the Sales Playbook with you. They write the Accountabilities Document for the next hire. They run the pipeline reviews until your Sales Manager is ready to take them over. Cost is a fraction of a VP, usually $7K to $15K a month, no benefits, no equity, no long-term risk.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Option 3. A Full-Time Head of Sales / VP of Sales</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Best for: You're approaching or above $10M ARR. You have an active Sales Playbook, KPIs that get tracked weekly, and a team of at least 4 to 6 reps. You can absorb a $250K+ all-in compensation package and a six-month ramp without breaking the business.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">At this stage, a full-time VP of Sales is the right hire. Below this stage, it almost never is. The wrong-stage VP is the founder mistake I see most often. Six months in, you've burned through $100K to $200K, your VP is frustrated because the foundation isn't there, you're still jumping in to save deals, and nothing has changed. They leave. You start over. Sometimes you start over twice.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(242, 161, 0); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">"You don't hire a general to invent the army. border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">What to Look For in Any Sales Leader You Hire</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Once you've decided which option fits your stage, the next risk is hiring the wrong person inside that option. The criteria are different for each role, but four traits show up on every successful sales leader I've worked with or hired in fifty years.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">1. They show up with a playbook, not a request for one</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A real sales leader has built systems before. They walk into the interview with a point of view on what your Sales Playbook should look like. They've already thought about how your ICP gets qualified, what your pipeline stages should be, what your KPI cadence should look like. If they're showing up asking you what your sales process is and waiting to be told, they're not a leader. They're a follower with a fancier title.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">2. They interview you as hard as you interview them</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A real sales leader asks for your quota attainment numbers. Your win rate. Your win-loss notes from the last six months. Your churn data. Your comp plan. If they're not interviewing you, they don't care whether they can win at your company. They just want the title and the income. That's the leader who quits at month seven when they realize the foundation wasn't there.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">3. They want the Accountabilities Document signed before week one</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">An Accountabilities Document is a single page with three to five measurable outcomes for the first six months. A real sales leader insists on writing it together with you before they accept the role. Not because they're being difficult. Because they know if you can't agree on what success looks like in writing, you'll never agree on it in conversation either. The leader who doesn't push for this document is the leader who'll blame you when you can't define what they were supposed to do.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">4. They've done it at your stage, not three stages ahead</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Be careful of the candidate from the unicorn. They built a team at a Series C company with a marketing engine, an SDR layer, brand recognition, and a thousand inbound leads a week. None of that exists at your company. Hire someone who has built a sales motion from scratch in a $1M to $10M ARR environment. They've felt your pain. They know what works without infrastructure because they didn't have any either.</p><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">A Story I Still Think About From My Own Career</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A few years back, a CEO I'd just met asked me to step into his company as a Fractional Sales Manager. The company was in a technology space I'd never sold into. I almost said no. I gave myself three reasons why I shouldn't take it.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">I don't know the market. I don't know the buyers. I have no experience with the technology they sell.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">In the end, I convinced myself if they had enough confidence in me, I'd be a fool to doubt myself. So, I jumped in. After a couple of months, I'd gone from their Fractional Sales Manager to their Fractional Vice President of Sales and was then asked to join their leadership team.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">I'm not telling you this story to toot my own horn. I'm telling you because of what happened inside the company. The CEO had been about to hire a full-time VP of Sales at $260K base. He'd already done two rounds of interviews. The market knowledge he was hiring for, the one that worried me on day one, turned out to be the thing nobody actually needed in the first ninety days. What the company needed was a playbook, a cadence, a KPI scoreboard, and somebody senior who'd built sales motions before.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">By month four, attainment was up. By month six, the CEO told me he'd dodged a bullet. Not because the VP candidate was bad. Because the company wasn't ready for one yet. The full-time hire would have spent the first nine months doing exactly what I did in three. Then they would have left because the foundation still wasn't built.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(242, 161, 0); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">"A VP of Sales amplifies what you already have. If you have chaos, they'll give you expensive, amplified chaos. If you have a system, they can scale it to the moon."</blockquote><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">How a Fractional Sales Leader Bridges You to the Right Hire</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The honest answer to "when should I hire a sales leader?" for most $1M to $10M ARR founders is, "you should hire one now, and the one you should hire is a Fractional Sales Leader." Not because that's my role. Because at your stage, that's the role the math actually supports.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A Fractional Sales Leader bridges three things in your first ninety days. First, the Sales Playbook gets built and used, not stored on a Google Drive nobody opens. Second, the Accountabilities Document for your next full-time hire gets written so when you do bring on a Sales Manager or eventually a VP, they walk in knowing what success looks like. Third, the founder gets back the hours that were going to weekly pipeline reviews, rep coaching sessions, and reactive deal-by-deal closing.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">By month twelve, one of two things has happened. Either you've grown enough that a full-time VP of Sales is now the right next hire, and the foundation is built so they can succeed. Or you've decided the Fractional Sales Leader model is the one that fits your business indefinitely, and you've kept it. Both outcomes save you the $250,000 landmine of hiring a full-time VP at the wrong stage.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><a href="https://louiebernstein.com/articles/vp-sales-vs-fractional-sales-leader" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 20px 24px; border-radius: 8px; display: flex; align-items: center; gap: 12px; text-decoration-line: none; background: rgb(244, 246, 250); border-left: 4px solid rgb(14, 77, 92);"><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;"><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 4px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700; letter-spacing: 0.1em; text-transform: uppercase; display: block; color: rgb(14, 77, 92);">Related Reading</span><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.35; color: rgb(14, 77, 92);">VP of Sales vs. Fractional Sales Leader: The $250k Decision You Can't Afford to Get Wrong &rarr;</span></div></a><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">Frequently Asked Questions</h2><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: At what revenue should a B2B company hire its first Head of Sales?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">There's no universal number. The titles are often interchangeable at this stage. A Head of Sales is sometimes a slightly more senior individual contributor with management responsibility, and a VP of Sales typically carries more strategic and budget authority. In a 4 to 10-person sales team, either title describes the same job. That's the honest answer. They'll spend the first three months learning your product, your buyers, and your team. The next three months building or rebuilding your sales process. By month six they're contributing. By month twelve they're producing. If you're at $1M to $10M ARR, you're probably paying $250K+ all-in for those twelve months while the foundation gets built. That same foundation can be built in ninety days by a Fractional Sales Leader for a fraction of the cost.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: Should I promote my top salesperson to Head of Sales?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Usually no, and I'd say almost never. Selling and managing sellers are different jobs. Your top rep is great at selling, which means they have the patterns that work for them, not the patterns they can transfer to four other people. Promoting them does two things at once. You lose your best individual contributor, and you usually don't gain a great manager. If the top rep genuinely wants to manage and you have the time to coach them through it, the Fractional Sales Leader model is the right bridge. They can mentor the new manager while you preserve your best seller's production for the first six months.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How much does a Fractional Sales Leader cost compared to a full-time VP of Sales?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">A Fractional Sales Leader typically runs $7K to $15K per month with no benefits, no equity grant, and no long-term commitment.
