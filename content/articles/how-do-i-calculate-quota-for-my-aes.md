As if quota is a knob you turn. Set it high and you'll get more revenue. Set it low and the rep coasts. Pick the right number and you win.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">That's not how it works. Quota isn't the input. It's step two of a five-step model, and if you don't run the whole model, you'll either starve your growth or blow up your budget. I've watched both happen to good companies.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Here's the approach I use when I sit down with a founder at $1M–$10M ARR and we figure out what their sales org actually needs to look like next year.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">Start With the Revenue You Need, Not the Quota</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The first number isn't quota. It's the net-new revenue you have to close in the next twelve months. And here's the part founders skip: it's more than you're closing now.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">You're not building a team to hold the line. You're building it to grow. So the target is next year's number minus what your existing base renews on its own. If you want to add $10m in net-new, that $10m is the foundation of everything that follows. Every other number in the model gets derived from it.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Get this number wrong and the whole model is wrong. I'd rather a founder spend an hour being honest about the real growth target than spend a quarter hiring against a target they invented to feel good.</p><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">Set a Quota Your Deal Size Can Actually Support</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Now, and only now, you calculate a reasonable, attainable quota for your closers. This is generally derivative of your deal size. The bigger your average deal, the more one rep can carry. The smaller your deal, the more deals they have to grind out to hit the same number, and there's a ceiling on how many calls a human can run.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Small deals</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">If you sell small, your reps may struggle to close $400k a year. Industry data backs this up. A rep working $3k–$10k deals typically lands around $400k–$450k in annual quota, because the deal count required to go higher just isn't humanly sustainable.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Mid-size deals</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Middle of the market, think $20k–$40k deals, and a rep can reasonably carry $600k–$700k, sometimes up to $800k. This is where most of my founders live, and a $600k quota is the number I anchor to when I build a model for a company at this stage.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Big deals</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Bigger deals, six figures and up, and a field rep can carry $1m, $1.5m, sometimes more. Fewer deals, longer cycles, but each one moves the number meaningfully.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">There's a sanity check I run on every quota number: the quota-to-OTE ratio. Across B2B, a healthy ratio sits between 4x and 5x. A mid-market AE on $120k OTE carrying a $600k quota is right in the sweet spot at 5x. If your quota implies a ratio below 3x, you're overpaying for the revenue. Above 6x or 7x and you've set a number nobody will hit, which is its own kind of expensive.</p><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 0px; border-radius: 10px; overflow: hidden; box-shadow: rgba(58, 42, 82, 0.12) 0px 4px 20px;"><img src="https://ckffpnbnpvwmecwzovxf.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/media/articles/1782508046140_quota-5step-model.png" alt="The 5-step AE quota and headcount model: revenue target, AE quota by deal size, yield factor, the load of managers and SDRs, and specialization tax" style="width:100%;display:block;border-radius:10px;" /></div><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left-color: rgb(242, 103, 78); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">Quota is step two of five. If you stop here, you've priced one seat. You haven't sized a team.</blockquote><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">Apply a Yield Factor for the Reps Who Won't Make It</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Here's where the model gets honest. Not every rep works out. And the ones who do still take time to scale. So you can't plan as if every seat produces full quota. You have to discount it.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">To stay conservative, I assume a 75% yielded quota. Multiply your raw headcount by that factor, or divide your target by it, and you get a number that survives contact with reality.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Is 75% pessimistic? It isn't. CaptivateIQ's 2025 benchmarks put the industry average at roughly 74% of quota attained. So even my "conservative" number is just the actual average. And it gets worse at the seat level: in software, only about 41% of reps reach full quota in a given period, and across the average sales org, roughly half the team is short of their number in any given quarter. Plan for 100% and you're planning for a team that statistically doesn't exist.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Then there's ramp. A new AE doesn't close on contact. The Bridge Group's SaaS benchmark puts average ramp at about 5.3 months, with a median of six, and deals above $50k ACV stretching past nine. A rep you hire in Q1 is barely contributing until Q3. The yield factor absorbs all of that: the misses, the attrition, the ramp. It's the difference between a model that looks good on a slide and one that holds up in December.</p><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">Add the Load: Managers, SDRs, and the Cost Centers</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Your closers don't operate in a vacuum. Around them sits the load: the VPs, the directors, the sales ops leaders, the sales engineers, the SDRs feeding the top of the funnel. These people are essential, and for the purposes of this model, they're cost centers. They don't carry a closing quota, but you still have to pay them and count them.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Two ratios I use to size the load. Assume one sales manager for every eight sales professionals. That isn't a number I made up. SBI and most span-of-control research land on 8:1 as best practice for a direct sales force, tightening to 6:1 in enterprise and loosening to 10:1 or 12:1 in high-velocity SMB. And assume one head of sales development for every ten SDRs.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Span of control isn't just an org-chart nicety, either. The research is clear that when you stretch managers too thin, coaching drops and ramp slows. Tightening span from twelve reps to nine measurably lifts the percentage of reps hitting 100%. So the load isn't overhead you tolerate. It's the infrastructure that makes the quota attainable in the first place.</p><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">The Specialization Tax</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The more specialized your sales process, the more bodies you need. SDRs to prospect, BDRs to qualify, AEs to close, SEs to handle the technical win, CSMs to retain. In theory, specialization raises quotas enough to pay for itself, because each role does one thing well. In practice, it rarely nets out clean. It usually adds about 20% to the model.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">For a founder at $1M–$10M ARR, the lesson is restraint. Don't specialize before your deal economics can fund it. A common, expensive failure I see is a founder copying the org chart of a company three stages ahead, layering in roles the revenue can't support yet, and watching efficiency crater. Build the specialization the deal size earns, not the one the blog post recommends.</p><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">Run the Whole Model: Why $10M Needs 30 People</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Let's put it together with real numbers. Say you want to add $10m in net-new revenue next year. Your deals are about $25k in ACV. And we've agreed a $600k quota is reasonable and attainable for that deal size.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Watch what the model produces.</p><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 0px; border-radius: 10px; overflow: hidden; box-shadow: rgba(58, 42, 82, 0.12) 0px 4px 20px;"><img src="https://ckffpnbnpvwmecwzovxf.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/media/articles/1782508054531_quota-10m-headcount.png" alt="Headcount build-up for $10M net-new revenue: 16 AEs plus 4 SDRs, 1 VP and 2 managers equals 22 planned heads, divided by 0.75 yield equals 30 sales professionals" style="width:100%;display:block;border-radius:10px;" /></div><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Sixteen fully scaled AEs just to do the closing work ($10m divided by $600k). Four SDRs to screen and feed them. One VP to run the function. Two managers to keep the span of control sane. That's 22 heads on paper. Now divide by your 0.75 yield factor, because a meaningful share of those seats won't produce full quota, and you land at 30.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left-color: rgb(242, 103, 78); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">Thirty sales professionals to add a net $10m in new revenue. Even with a healthy $600k quota. That's the number the "what quota should I give my AE?" question never sees coming.</blockquote><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Most founders, when they imagine adding $10m, picture maybe a dozen reps. The model says 30 sales professionals once you account for the load and the yield. That gap, between what founders imagine and what the math requires, is the single most common reason a growth plan misses. Not effort. Not talent. Arithmetic.</p><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">Why Sales Efficiency Drops as You Scale</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">There's one more thing this model exposes, and it's the part nobody warns founders about. Sales efficiency drops over time, even if you do everything right.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">In the early days, you don't need much management. Your effective yield is higher because you hand-picked your first reps. Your magic number looks great. Then you scale. You add the management layer. You add SDRs and ops. Your yield regresses toward the mean as you hire past your personal network. Just the act of scaling, the load and the yield drag alone, will knock your sales efficiency down 30% or more even if everything else stays constant.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">This isn't a theory. The B2B SaaS magic number has declined by roughly 57%, and a big driver is that go-to-market teams now cost nearly twice as much to support the same selling capacity, because spend has shifted toward all those non-closing roles. The early performers regressing toward the mean as the team grows is a documented statistical effect, not a hiring mistake.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">So when your CFO asks why the new reps don't look as productive as the first two, this is the answer. It's not that the team got worse. It's that scaling itself carries a tax, and a real model prices it in from the start. This is exactly the kind of math I build with founders before they make the hires, not after they've missed the number. I plan on 75% yielded quota, meaning I assume the team produces 75% of its theoretical full capacity. You divide your headcount target by 0.75 to find how many people you actually need to hire. It's grounded in data: industry quota attainment averages around 74%, and average SaaS ramp time runs over five months, so a seat rarely produces a full year of full quota.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How many sales managers do I need?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">The benchmark is one manager for every eight sales professionals, tightening toward 6:1 in complex enterprise selling and loosening toward 10:1 or 12:1 in high-velocity SMB.
