margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">No founder sets out to be the bottleneck. The path is the same in almost every company. You closed the first ten deals because nobody else could explain the product the way you could. You closed the next twenty because the early reps you hired needed help on the hard ones. By deal fifty you had built a sales motion so deep inside your own head that nobody else could run it without your fingerprints on every conversation.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">That motion was your competitive advantage when revenue was a million dollars a year. It is your ceiling now. Every conversation that has to route through you is a conversation that has to wait for your calendar. Every rep deal you have to rescue is a deal that did not close on its own. Every weekend you spend cleaning up the pipeline is a weekend the rest of the business did not get from you.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The reps you hired are not bad. They are running without a playbook. There is no documented version of what you do. There is no CRM stage they can move a deal into without asking you. There are no qualification rules they can lean on to kill a bad deal early. Without that infrastructure, every rep ends up running the version of the sales motion they remember from their last job, which is not your motion at all.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">This is the moment most founders look at the org chart and decide they need a VP of Sales. The instinct is correct. The execution is almost always wrong. You do not need a VP. You need the system a VP would build, installed, and someone running it for nine to twelve months while the company gets used to having one. That is what a Fractional Sales Leader is.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">What Doing Everything Actually Costs You</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Pull out a calculator. A founder of a $3M ARR business with a reasonable equity position is worth roughly $1,442 an hour to the company. That is not a vanity number — it is what an honest cost-per-hour looks like when you build it up from comp, equity, and the value of the decisions only you can make. Every hour you spend selling is an hour priced at that rate.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Twenty-five hours a week of selling is $36,058 a week. Almost $145,000 a month. Just over $1.8 million a year. That is the line item nobody puts on the P&amp;L because the founder is not cutting themselves a check for it. The cost shows up as the things that did not happen — the partnership you did not pursue, the product roadmap that slipped, the hiring decision that took six weeks instead of two.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Then layer in the missed opportunities. Eight deals a month walk away because nobody followed up on the right cadence, the demo got rescheduled three times, the proposal sat in the founder's inbox over a long weekend. At a $5,000 first-year value per customer, that is $40,000 a month — almost half a million a year — in revenue that should have been yours and went somewhere else.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">And then the hire-the-wrong-VP risk. The average bad VP of Sales hire at this stage costs about $315,000 over the first six months alone — base, benefits, recruiting, the two reps who quit because the new VP did not fit, and the months of pipeline that died waiting for the new leader to figure out what they had inherited. Add it up across the three columns and the total cost of staying where you are dwarfs anything a fractional engagement would cost.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 3px solid rgb(232, 97, 10); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px; background: rgb(255, 247, 241);">"Your time is the most expensive resource in your company. Not in a slide deck. In your CRM. In your inbox. In the room with your reps. The work is unglamorous and concrete.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">They write the playbook you keep meaning to write</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Twenty to forty pages. The Ideal Customer Profile with disqualifying criteria, not just qualifying ones. The discovery script you actually use, not the one in the deck. Every recurring objection and the response that works. The proposal template. The closing cadence. By the end of week three a new rep can run your sales motion without ever being in the room with you.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">They build a CRM that tells the truth</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Five pipeline stages, ten required fields, and action-based exit criteria for each stage — not hope-based ones. A deal does not move forward because the rep had a meeting. It moves forward because the buyer confirmed the budget, the timeline, and the decision-maker. <b>If it is not in the CRM, it never happened.</b></p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">They run the weekly cadence so you do not have to</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A forty-five-minute pipeline review every Monday. Every open deal named out loud. Every stalled deal challenged. Every forecasted close defended with evidence. You are welcome to attend. After about week six most founders stop, because the meeting works without them.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">They coach the reps you already have and source the ones you do not</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The reps who can grow into the playbook get coached up. The ones who cannot, get managed out — honestly and with enough runway to land somewhere they will succeed. New hires get sourced through the fractional leader's network and run through a hiring process that pattern-matches them against the playbook, not against the founder's gut.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><a href="https://louiebernstein.com/what-are-the-hidden-costs-of-founder-led-sales" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 20px 24px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(232, 97, 10); border-radius: 0px 8px 8px 0px; display: flex; align-items: center; gap: 12px; text-decoration: none; background: rgb(250, 250, 250);"><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;"><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 4px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700; letter-spacing: 0.1em; text-transform: uppercase; display: block; color: rgb(232, 97, 10); font-size: 0.7rem;">Related Reading</span><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.35; color: rgb(27, 58, 107); font-weight: 700;">What Are the Hidden Costs of Founder-Led Sales? →</span></div></a><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">The First 90 Days: What Actually Happens</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Most founders ask one of two questions when they hear about a fractional engagement. Here is the day-by-day truth.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">Days 1–30: Diagnose, document, and clean the room</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Week one is shadowing — listening to your sales calls, watching your inbox, reading your CRM. Week two is extraction: the playbook gets drafted in a working session with you, on the calls you have already had. Week three is the CRM rebuild — five stages, ten fields, exit criteria written down. Week four the new system goes live with your reps. You are still on every important call. You are no longer the only person managing the pipeline.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">Days 31–60: Run the cadence, cut the chaos</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The Monday pipeline review is up and running. Reps are using the playbook on real calls and the fractional leader is coaching them after each one. The forecast is honest for the first time in a year. Bad deals are getting killed instead of dragged. You start noticing you have not been pulled into a deal that was not yours in two weeks. By day sixty you are spending fewer than ten hours a week on sales.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">Days 61–90: Hand off, scale up, plan the exit</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Your remaining hours move toward strategic deals only — the lighthouse accounts, the partnership-shaped opportunities, the customer expansions that need the founder's name on them.
