It's the function with the most emotional volatility and the least natural off-switch, and you've made yourself the off-switch. It's a handful of deliberate moves that shift load off you and onto a system. Here are the four I'd start with.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">1. Get your sales process out of your head and onto paper</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Right now the playbook lives in one place: your head. That's why nobody can run a deal without you. Write down how you actually sell. The moment it's transferable, it stops being your job alone.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">2. Then they end up re-closing every deal anyway, more exhausted than before. Don't do that. Build an Accountabilities Document that spells out exactly what the role owns, the activity it's responsible for, and the outcomes it's measured on. Clarity is what lets you actually let go, because you can hold someone to something specific instead of hovering over everything.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">3. Protect the work only you can do</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">There are a few things in your company that genuinely require the founder: vision, key partnerships, the biggest strategic bets. Most founders are stunned by how short the "only me" list actually is.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">4.
