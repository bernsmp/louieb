The prospect is telling their story. You are asking questions. There is energy in the room because you are uncovering things together. Pain gets named. Stakes get articulated. By the end of a strong discovery call, both parties feel it. The problem is real, the cost is real, and there is a shared understanding of what is at stake.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Then a week goes by. You know what the problem is. You know how long it has been happening. You know what it is costing them. You know who has to sign off. You know what they have already tried and why it did not work.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">That is not background information. That is the most powerful material you have going into the second call. And the way you use it is not by summarizing it back in your own words. That you hired two salespeople in that time and neither one stuck. And that if something doesn't change in the next six months, you are going to miss your growth target for the third year in a row. Does that still reflect where things stand?"</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The first version confirms you were listening. The second version puts the prospect back inside the problem. That is the difference between a recap and a reactivation.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Use their words. They re-inhabit the feeling. The urgency that faded over the past two weeks comes back into focus. The first call was about listening and learning. This call is about demonstrating that you heard everything — and that what you are about to propose is a direct response to it. Every section of the call flows from that purpose.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 1: Confirm the agenda and check for anything new</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Open the same way you opened the discovery call. State the purpose. Tell them what you plan to cover. Then ask a question you do not ask enough: "Before I get into what I put together, has anything changed since we last spoke?"</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">This is not small talk. Business moves fast. A key person may have left. A budget may have been frozen. A competing priority may have come in. The problem you built your solution around may have shifted. Finding that out in the first two minutes is infinitely better than finding it out twenty minutes into your presentation.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Ask it every time. Even when you are confident nothing has changed.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 2: Restate the problem — fully</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">This is the step most salespeople rush or skip. They do not skip it entirely — they do a version of it. But they do it at the surface level. They summarize the problem in their own words, check it off the agenda, and move into the presentation.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">That is not enough.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The restatement has to do two things. First, it has to be accurate enough that the prospect thinks, "Yes, that is exactly what I said." Second, it has to be specific enough that the weight of the problem lands again. Not as a memory of a past conversation. As a present reality.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Here is language that works: "Before I walk you through what I put together, I want to make sure I am presenting the right solution. Let me reflect back what I heard, and you tell me if anything needs to be corrected or added."</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Then lay it out. The problem. The duration. The cost. The things they have already tried. The stakes if nothing changes. All of it. In their words wherever possible.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">When you finish, ask: "Does that accurately reflect where things stand? Is there anything you would add or change?"</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">That question does something important. It gives the prospect their first agreement of the call. They say yes, that is right. And the first yes is always the easiest one to get — which means the next one will be slightly easier too.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 3: Bridge from their problem to your solution</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Do not start the presentation by describing what you do. Start by connecting what you do to what they told you. Every feature, every capability, every proof point should be introduced as a direct response to something the prospect said in the discovery call.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Not: "We offer a twelve-week engagement that includes a sales audit, playbook development, and pipeline management."</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">But: "You told me your reps are doing it differently every time because there is no documented process. The first thing we build in week one is the playbook — so there is one way, your way, written down and followed."</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The prospect should be able to hear their own words in your solution. When they do, the solution stops feeling like a pitch and starts feeling like an answer. That is a different experience entirely, and it changes how they evaluate what they are hearing.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 4: Invite objections before they surface</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Most salespeople treat objections as interruptions. They present, the prospect pushes back, and the rep shifts into defense mode. That dynamic puts you on opposite sides of the table.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">There is a better approach. Once you have walked through the solution, ask for the objections directly. "What questions do you have?" is fine. Better is: "What concerns do you have about whether this will work for your situation?"</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">That phrasing signals that you are not fragile. You are not defending a pitch. You are diagnosing fit. The prospect experiences you as confident and consultative rather than eager and defensive. And you get the real objections on the table where you can address them, rather than letting them sit unspoken until after the call.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 5: Close to the next step</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The second call ends the same way the first one did. With a specific next step, agreed to on the call, with a date attached. What that next step looks like depends on where you are in the process — a proposal review, a call with additional stakeholders, a contract discussion — but it must be concrete and it must be calendared before you hang up.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">If the prospect will not commit to a next step at the end of the second call, that is important information. It means either the urgency is not as real as it felt in the discovery call, or there is a concern that has not surfaced yet. Not a skim. A real review, with specific attention to the language the prospect used.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Before every second call, your reps should be able to answer these questions from memory:</p><ul style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px; list-style: none;"><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 8px 0px 8px 32px; position: relative; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237); line-height: 1.55;">What is the specific problem — in the prospect's exact words, not a paraphrase?</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 8px 0px 8px 32px; position: relative; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237); line-height: 1.55;">How long has this problem been happening, and what have they already tried to fix it?</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 8px 0px 8px 32px; position: relative; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237); line-height: 1.55;">What does this problem cost them — in revenue, in time, in the founder's own bandwidth?</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 8px 0px 8px 32px; position: relative; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237); line-height: 1.55;">What happens if they do nothing about it for the next twelve months?</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 8px 0px 8px 32px; position: relative; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237); line-height: 1.55;">Who makes the final decision, and who else needs to be involved or informed?</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 8px 0px 8px 32px; position: relative; border-bottom-width: medium; border-bottom-style: none; border-bottom-color: currentcolor; line-height: 1.55;">Is there anything unresolved from the discovery call — a question asked, a concern raised, a detail left unclear?</li></ul><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">That last one is critical. Unresolved items from the discovery call do not disappear. They resurface — usually at the worst possible moment, mid-presentation, when someone asks a question that derails everything. Identify them before the call and address them at the start, before you get into the presentation.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">I have one more prep practice I require of my reps before a high-stakes second call: role-play the restatement. Not the full presentation. Just the two minutes where they reflect the problem back. It sounds simple. It is not. Most reps are too vague, too quick, or too much in their own words the first time they try it. Practice it until it is sharp, specific, and sounds like it came from the prospect's mouth — not yours.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-width: 1px medium medium; border-style: solid none none; border-color: rgb(226, 230, 237) currentcolor currentcolor; border-image: initial;"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">What to Do When the Problem Has Changed</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Sometimes you do the restatement and the prospect corrects you. Something shifted since the discovery call. A budget got cut. A new priority came in. The problem you built the solution around is no longer the main problem.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">This is not a failure. This is the system working.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">If you had skipped the restatement and gone straight into the presentation, you would have spent twenty minutes presenting a solution to a problem the prospect had mentally moved on from. The call would have felt off and you would not have known why.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">When the problem changes, you have two options. If the change is minor, acknowledge it, adjust your framing, and continue. If the change is significant, say so directly: "That changes things enough that I want to make sure what I present is still the right fit. Can we take ten minutes to get current on where things stand before I walk you through what I put together?"</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Most prospects will respect that. It shows you are not there to push a solution — you are there to solve the right problem. That is a meaningful distinction, and prospects feel it.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 3px solid rgb(232, 97, 10); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">A solution presented to the wrong problem is not a solution. It is a pitch. Know the difference before you start talking.</blockquote><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-width: 1px medium medium; border-style: solid none none; border-color: rgb(226, 230, 237) currentcolor currentcolor; border-image: initial;"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">The Follow-Up That Keeps the Deal Moving</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Within twenty-four hours of the second call, send a follow-up that covers three things. First, a brief version of the problem as you both agreed it stands — this keeps the urgency from fading again. Second, a summary of the key points from the solution you presented, tied directly to the specific problems they described. Third, the confirmed next step, including the date and time.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Do not write a pitch. Do not send a brochure. The follow-up to the second call should read like a summary of an agreement you have been building together — because that is what it is.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">One additional step that separates the best salespeople I have managed from the average ones: update the CRM immediately after the second call with any changes to the problem, any new stakeholders identified, any objections raised, and the confirmed next step. If that information lives only in the rep's head, it is one bad week away from being lost entirely. Put it in writing. Every time.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-width: 1px medium medium; border-style: solid none none; border-color: rgb(226, 230, 237) currentcolor currentcolor; border-image: initial;"><a href="https://louiebernstein.com/articles/how-to-conduct-a-discovery-call" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 20px 24px; border-radius: 8px; display: flex; align-items: center; gap: 12px; text-decoration: none;"><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;"><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 4px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700; letter-spacing: 0.1em; text-transform: uppercase; display: block;">Related Reading</span><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.35;">How to Run a Discovery Call With a New Prospect →</span></div></a><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">Frequently Asked Questions</h2><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How long should the restatement take on a second call?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Two to three minutes. It should be long enough to cover the problem fully — the issue, the duration, the cost, and the stakes — but short enough that it does not feel like a recap meeting. The goal is to bring the prospect back to the emotional reality of the problem, not to recite a transcript of the discovery call. If your reps are restating in thirty seconds, they are being too vague. If they are going five minutes, they are covering too much ground and should tighten it.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: What if the prospect seems impatient with the restatement and just wants to hear the solution?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Do it anyway, but do it faster. A prospect who seems impatient at the start of a second call is usually just eager. That eagerness is not a signal to skip steps — it is a signal that urgency is high. Use that energy. Do a tighter, sharper version of the restatement: "I want to spend sixty seconds making sure I have the problem right before I show you what I put together." Then do it in sixty seconds. The prospect will not object to that. And you will have done the critical work of confirming alignment before you present.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: Should the second call always include a presentation, or are there situations where more discovery is needed?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">If you left the discovery call with gaps — questions you did not get answered, stakeholders you have not spoken to, parts of the problem that felt unclear — the second call is not the right time to present. Use it to fill those gaps first. Presenting a solution on incomplete discovery is one of the most reliable ways to lose a deal you should have won. Better to spend a second call doing deeper discovery than to present a solution that misses the mark and has to be rebuilt from scratch.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: What if the prospect brings up a concern mid-presentation that you did not hear in discovery?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Stop and address it before continuing. Do not log the concern mentally and plan to come back to it later — that almost never works. Acknowledge it directly: "That is an important concern. Let me address it now before we go further." Handle it fully, confirm the prospect is satisfied, and then continue. A concern that is noted and deferred tends to grow. A concern that is addressed in the moment tends to close. This is why inviting objections proactively before they surface — as covered in Step 4 above — is worth making a habit.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How do I coach a rep who consistently skips or rushes the restatement?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Role-play it. Put yourself in the prospect's seat and ask the rep to restate the problem from the last deal they worked. When they give you a vague surface-level version, push back: "That is what I said — but does it sound like me? Does it use my words?" Make them do it again until it is specific, accurate, and in the prospect's language. Then pull a recording of a recent second call and listen to the transition from opening to presentation together. Most reps can hear the gap clearly when they listen to themselves. Hearing it is usually enough to change the behavior — if you make them listen.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: What is the biggest sign that a second call went poorly, even if the prospect seemed engaged?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">No commitment to a next step. A prospect who sat through the full presentation, asked questions, said positive things, and then ended the call with "let me think about it and get back to you" is not an engaged prospect. They are a polite one. Engagement without a next step means the urgency was not high enough, the fit was not clear enough, or there is a concern still sitting unspoken. When you get to the end of a second call and the prospect will not commit to a specific next step, stop and ask directly: "Is there something that would need to be true for you to feel ready to move forward?" The answer to that question will tell you everything about where the deal actually stands.</p></div><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-width: 1px medium medium; border-style: solid none none; border-color: rgb(226, 230, 237) currentcolor currentcolor; border-image: initial;"><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 48px 0px 32px; padding: 40px 36px; border-radius: 10px; text-align: center;"><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 12px; padding: 0px;">Your reps are running second calls right now.</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 24px; padding: 0px;">The question is whether they are reactivating the pain or just referencing it.
