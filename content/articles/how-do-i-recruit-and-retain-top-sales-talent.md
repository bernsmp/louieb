And if I had to pick the single hardest job a founder takes on between $1M and $10M, it's this one. Not building product. Not raising money. Not even closing deals. Hiring and keeping the right salespeople.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Most founders walk into sales hiring with the same instincts they used to hire their first engineer or their first marketer. Read the resume. Like the person. Hire the one with the best story. Then wonder six months later why nothing's closing and why the rep is already updating their LinkedIn.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Sales hiring doesn't work that way. Sales reps are paid to be persuasive. They're paid to handle objections. They're paid to make you believe. That means the interview is the easiest part of the job for them. The hard part starts after you've signed the offer letter.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Here's the playbook I use with the founders I work with. It covers the full talent lifecycle: writing a job description that filters for the right people, an interview process that exposes who can actually sell, an onboarding plan that ramps them in 90 days, and a retention approach that keeps your A-players from walking out the door.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-width: 1px medium medium; border-style: solid none none; border-color: rgb(226, 230, 237) currentcolor currentcolor; border-image: initial;"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">Why Most Founder Sales Hires Fail</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Before I get into the how, you have to understand the why. Because the way most founders hire sales is broken before they ever post the job.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">They hire out of desperation.</strong>&nbsp;The founder is burned out from doing all the selling. Revenue is flat. They need a rep yesterday. So they take the first person who looks the part and can hold a coherent conversation. That's not hiring. That's emergency room triage. The reps you hire in panic are almost never the reps you'd hire on a calm Tuesday.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">They hire someone they like.</strong>&nbsp;Likability is not a hiring criterion. It's a tiebreaker between two qualified candidates. I've watched founders hire people they wanted to grab a beer with, then act surprised when those people couldn't run a discovery call. Liking a candidate tells you they're pleasant. It doesn't tell you they can sell.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">They hire a clone of themselves.</strong>&nbsp;You closed deals by being the founder. You know the product cold. You believe in it. You can answer any question because you built the thing. A new hire has none of that. If you hire someone and expect them to sell exactly the way you sell, you're going to be disappointed every time.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">They hire from big-company resumes.</strong>&nbsp;The rep who hit quota at Salesforce or Oracle is not necessarily the rep who can win for you. Those companies have leads handed to them, brand recognition that opens doors, and entire support teams. At $1M to $10M, your new hire is going to have to dig wells. Most enterprise reps have never picked up a shovel.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Every one of those mistakes is preventable. But you have to slow down enough to do the work upfront.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-width: 1px medium medium; border-style: solid none none; border-color: rgb(226, 230, 237) currentcolor currentcolor; border-image: initial;"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">Step 1: Write a Job Description That Filters In and Filters Out</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The job description is not a marketing piece. It's a filter. A good one attracts the people who'll thrive in your environment and quietly tells everyone else to apply somewhere else.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Most founders write job descriptions that read like every other one. "Fast-paced environment. Rocket ship company. Looking for a hungry self-starter." That language attracts everyone. Which means it attracts no one in particular.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Here's what a strong sales JD does instead:</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">It names your stage.</strong>&nbsp;Say it plainly. "We're a $3M ARR company with one founder doing most of the selling, and we're hiring a rep who can take that off my plate." That sentence does more filtering than a page of buzzwords. The right candidate reads that and gets excited. The wrong candidate reads it and clicks away. Both outcomes save you time.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">It names what they'll actually do.</strong>&nbsp;Not "manage the sales cycle." Tell them. "You'll run 8 to 12 discovery calls a week, manage 30 to 40 open opportunities in HubSpot, present to founders and VPs of Sales as the buyer, and close deals between $15K and $80K." Now the candidate can self-assess. They either know how to do that or they don't.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">It names the comp.</strong>&nbsp;If you list a salary range, list a real one. "$80K base, $160K OTE, uncapped, paid monthly" tells the candidate exactly where they stand. Hiding the number tells them you're either embarrassed by it or you don't know what you're paying. Neither is a good look.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">It names the trade.</strong>&nbsp;Top reps care about two things beyond money: what they'll learn and where it'll take them. Tell them. "You'll work directly with the founder on every deal for the first 90 days. You'll see how a $3M company becomes a $10M company. You'll build a playbook from scratch. If you crush this role, the next seat is sales manager or VP of Sales as we grow." That's a recruiting pitch. Most JDs don't have one.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 3px solid rgb(232, 97, 10); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">A vague job description gets you 200 applicants and 5 you'd actually hire. A specific job description gets you 40 applicants and 15 you'd hire. Specificity is the lever.</blockquote><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-width: 1px medium medium; border-style: solid none none; border-color: rgb(226, 230, 237) currentcolor currentcolor; border-image: initial;"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">Step 2: An Interview Process That Exposes Who Can Actually Sell</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Resumes lie. References are coached. Confident answers are the easiest thing in the world to fake. If you want to know if someone can sell, you have to watch them sell.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Here's the four-stage process I use. This is where you find out if they did any homework. Did they look at your website? Do they know what you sell? Did they bring questions? If a sales rep can't be bothered to research the company they're interviewing with, they won't research the prospects they're supposed to close either. I cut a lot of candidates at this stage. So should you.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">Stage 2: Deal Walkthrough</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Ask them to walk you through a deal they recently closed. Not a highlight reel. The whole thing. The lead source. The first conversation. The decision-makers. The competition. The objections. The price. The close. The follow-up.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A real rep can do this for ten minutes without breaking a sweat because they lived it. A pretender will be vague, use buzzwords, and dodge specifics. Listen for the words. Did they say "we" a lot? That's a team player, or someone hiding behind the team. Did they say "I"? That's a closer, or an ego case. Ask them about the deal they lost. Their honest answer there tells you more than ten won-deal stories.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">Stage 3: The Selling Exercise</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">This is the one most founders skip. Don't. Ask them to sell you on the last product they sold. Not your product. Theirs. They know it cold. Then play hardball. Push back on price. Bring up a competitor. Ask why you should care. Throw an objection at them.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">In ten minutes you'll see exactly how they handle pressure, how they think on their feet, whether they listen or just keep talking, and whether they have a real process or are just charming. If you can't watch someone sell before you hire them, you're guessing. And in sales, guessing costs you a year.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">Stage 4: Backdoor References</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The references they hand you are coached. They'll say great things. That's not useful. What's useful is finding two or three people who worked with the candidate but weren't on their reference list. LinkedIn makes this easy. A former manager. A former peer. A former customer if you can find one. Reach out, mention you're considering this person for a role, and ask one question: "Would you hire them again?" The pause before the answer tells you everything.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-width: 1px medium medium; border-style: solid none none; border-color: rgb(226, 230, 237) currentcolor currentcolor; border-image: initial;"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">Step 3: An Onboarding Plan That Ramps Them in 90 Days</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">You can hire the best sales rep in the world and still watch them fail if you don't have an onboarding plan. Most founders don't have one. They hire the rep, hand them a login, give them the website to read, and tell them to start booking meetings by Friday. That's how you turn a great hire into a quick departure.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The first 90 days have to be structured, written down, and tied to specific outcomes. margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">No quota. No solo calls. Their job in the first 30 days is to learn. The product. The ideal customer profile. The CRM. The pricing. The objections. The competition. They should be shadowing every single sales call you take and writing down what they hear. By the end of week four, they should be able to tell you, in their own words, who you sell to, what problem you solve, and why you win. If they can't, the next 60 days are going to be rough.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">Days 31 through 60: Drive With a Co-Pilot</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Now they start running calls with you in the room. They open the meeting. They ask the discovery questions. They handle the objections. You sit quietly unless they ask for help or unless something is about to go off the rails. After every call, you debrief. What worked. What didn't. What they'd do differently. This is where coaching happens. Not in a quarterly review. In the thirty minutes right after the call ended.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px 10px; padding: 0px;">Days 61 through 90: Take the Wheel</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Now they run calls solo. You review the recording. You review the pipeline. You review the forecast. By day 90, they should have closed at least one deal. Not a giant one. Just one. The first close is the most important psychological moment of any new rep's tenure. It tells them, and tells you, that the system works.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">If day 90 comes and there's no closed deal and no real pipeline, you have to ask the hard question. Is it the rep, or is it the system? Sometimes it's the rep. Sometimes it's that you handed them no leads, no playbook, and no support. Be honest about which one it is before you blame them. *<b> Note</b>: the 90-day closed deal is not for Enterprise-sized deals. They can obviously take longer.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-width: 1px medium medium; border-style: solid none none; border-color: rgb(226, 230, 237) currentcolor currentcolor; border-image: initial;"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">Step 4: Retention Is Not About Perks</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">If you Google "sales retention," you'll get a thousand articles about culture, perks, and recognition swag. Ignore most of it. The reason top sales reps leave isn't because there's no ping pong table. They leave for three reasons, and you can control all three.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">Reason 1: The comp plan is broken.</strong>&nbsp;If your top rep is making less than they could make at a competitor, they're already on LinkedIn. Pay your A-players like A-players. Uncapped commissions. Paid monthly. Accelerators for over-quota performance. The math should make it impossible for a top rep to want to leave. If your math says "we can't afford to pay them that much," your math is wrong. You can't afford to lose them and rebuild from zero.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">Reason 2: They're not getting coached.</strong>&nbsp;Top reps want to get better. If you hire someone and then ignore them because they're hitting their number, you're losing them in slow motion. They will leave for the manager who actually pays attention. Weekly one-on-ones. Deal reviews. Role-plays. Honest feedback. If you don't have time to do this, you don't have a sales team. You have contractors.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">Reason 3: There's no path.</strong>&nbsp;Your rep is twenty-eight years old. They're not planning to be an account executive for the rest of their life. If they can't see where this role takes them in two years, they'll find a place where they can. Be explicit about the path. Sales manager. Senior AE. Sales director. Whatever it is, name it. And then actually promote people when they earn it. Promotion theater kills retention faster than bad comp.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 28px 0px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 3px solid rgb(232, 97, 10); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">Top sales reps don't quit jobs. They quit managers. If your A-player just walked, the first place to look is the mirror.</blockquote><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-width: 1px medium medium; border-style: solid none none; border-color: rgb(226, 230, 237) currentcolor currentcolor; border-image: initial;"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">When to Let Someone Go</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Not every hire works out. Even with a great process. The hardest skill in sales leadership isn't hiring. It's letting someone go before they cost you more than they already have.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The data I look at after 90 days is simple. Did they close a deal or is their pipeline real? Are they coachable? Do they take feedback and adjust, or do they nod and keep doing the same thing? If two of those four are red, the next 90 days won't fix it. The rep who hasn't ramped in 90 days almost never ramps in 180. I've seen it maybe twice in fifty years.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Founders drag this out because firing someone feels terrible. I get it. But every month you keep a non-performing rep is a month of lost pipeline, lost morale on the rest of the team, and lost time you could've spent finding the right person. The cost compounds.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Move fast. Be honest. Be kind in the delivery, ruthless in the timing. That's the rule.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-width: 1px medium medium; border-style: solid none none; border-color: rgb(226, 230, 237) currentcolor currentcolor; border-image: initial;"><a href="https://louiebernstein.com/how-to-hire-first-sales-rep" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 20px 24px; border-radius: 8px; display: flex; align-items: center; gap: 12px; text-decoration: none;"><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;"><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 4px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700; letter-spacing: 0.1em; text-transform: uppercase; display: block;">Related Reading</span><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.35;">How to Hire Your First Sales Rep →</span></div></a><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px 14px; padding: 0px;">Frequently Asked Questions</h2><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px;"><b>Q: How long should it take to hire a sales rep the right way?</b></p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Four to eight weeks from posting the job to signed offer. Faster than that and you're rushing the process. Slower than that and you're either too picky or your process has friction in it that's losing you good candidates. The biggest time sink is not the interviews. It's the gap between interviews where good candidates take other offers. Move fast between stages once a candidate is in the funnel.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px;"><b>Q: Should I hire one salesperson or two at the same time?</b></p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Two, almost every time. The biggest mistake I see at $1M to $3M is hiring one rep, watching them struggle for six months, and then deciding "sales hiring doesn't work for our company." It does work. You just don't have enough data with a single hire. Two reps gives you a comparison. You learn which one is the A-player, which one needs more support, and whether the issue is the person or the system. Hire two, give them both real shots, and keep the one who's ramping.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px;"><b>Q: What should I pay a sales rep at a $1M to $5M ARR company?</b></p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">For a mid-level account executive at this stage, expect $70K to $90K base, $140K to $180K on-target earnings, uncapped, with most of the commission tied to closed revenue. The exact split depends on deal size and cycle length. The principle: base should keep them alive, commission should make them rich. If your top rep can't make $200K plus in a great year, you don't have a comp plan, you have a salary plan. And salary plans don't produce sales results.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px;"><b>Q: My new rep is three months in and hasn't closed anything. Should I fire them?</b></p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Before you decide, answer four questions honestly. Did you give them an onboarding plan? Did you give them real leads, or did you ask them to build pipeline from zero? Did you coach them weekly? Are they coachable, meaning when you give them feedback do they actually change behavior? If the answers are yes, yes, yes, and no, the issue is the rep. If the answers are no, no, no, and yes, the issue is you. Be honest before you act.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px;"><b>Q: How do I keep a top sales rep from being recruited away?</b></p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">You can't fully. Recruiters will always be calling your A-players. What you can do is make leaving expensive for them. Pay them well. Coach them weekly. Give them a clear next step in their career. Make sure their commission checks are clean and on time, every time. And, this matters more than people think, have a real conversation with them at least once a quarter about how they're doing, what they want, and what's frustrating them. The reps who feel known don't leave for a $5K raise somewhere else.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px;"><b>Q: I'm a founder, not a sales manager.
