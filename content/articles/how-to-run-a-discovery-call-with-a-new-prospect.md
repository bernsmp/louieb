<!--StartFragment--><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 36px; padding: 24px 28px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(27, 58, 107); border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;"><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px; letter-spacing: 0.1em; text-transform: uppercase;">Key Takeaways:</h3><ul style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 0px; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-left: 0px; padding: 0px; list-style: none;"><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 6px 0px 6px 22px; line-height: 1.55; position: relative;">A discovery call is not a sales pitch. It is a diagnostic conversation. Your job is to ask questions and listen, not talk about your product.</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 6px 0px 6px 22px; line-height: 1.55; position: relative;">The three things you must uncover on every discovery call: the real problem, the cost of the problem, and who makes the decision to fix it.</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 6px 0px 6px 22px; line-height: 1.55; position: relative;">Founders and salespeople who talk more than they listen on discovery calls close fewer deals and they never understand why.</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 6px 0px 6px 22px; line-height: 1.55; position: relative;">Preparation before the call determines the quality of what you learn on the call. Walk in knowing their business. Walk out knowing their pain.</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 6px 0px 6px 22px; line-height: 1.55; position: relative;">Every discovery call must end with a clear next step that both parties have agreed to. A good call with no next step is a wasted call.</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 6px 0px 6px 22px; line-height: 1.55; position: relative;"><b>Disqualifying a bad prospect on a discovery call is as valuable as qualifying a great one. Both outcomes save you time.</b></li></ul></div><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">I have been on or coached thousands of discovery calls over fifty years in sales.&nbsp; And the most common mistake I see, at every level, from junior reps to founders closing million-dollar deals, is the same mistake every time.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><b>They show up to talk instead of to listen.</b></p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">They rehearse their pitch. They polish their deck. They memorize their feature list. And then they get on the call and spend forty-five minutes telling the prospect everything their company does, when all the prospect wanted was for someone to understand their problem.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A discovery call is not a presentation. It is not a demo. It is not your opportunity to impress anyone with what you know about your product. It is a diagnostic conversation. You are a doctor who has never seen this patient before. Your job is to ask the right questions, shut up, and listen to the answers.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Do that well, and closing the deal becomes the natural next step. Do it poorly, and no amount of follow-up or feature comparison will save you.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Here is everything I know about running a discovery call that actually works.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">Before the Call: Do the Work Nobody Else Does</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Most salespeople prepare for a discovery call by reviewing their own product. The best salespeople prepare by studying the prospect's business.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">There is a difference between a salesperson who opens a discovery call with "So, tell me a bit about what you do" and one who opens with "I saw your company just expanded into the Southeast. How is that affecting your sales team?" The first rep signals they did no homework. The second signals they are worth the prospect's time.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Before every discovery call, you should know the following:</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">Their business.</strong>&nbsp;What do they sell? Who do they sell to? What does their revenue model look like? What is their approximate size? Spend fifteen minutes on their website, LinkedIn, and any recent press before you pick up the phone.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">Their likely problem.</strong>&nbsp;Based on their stage and industry, what issues do companies like theirs typically have? If you sell to founders at $1M–$10M ARR, you already know their likely pain points before they tell you. Walk in with a hypothesis. Let the call confirm or correct it.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">How they found you.</strong>&nbsp;Did they come in through inbound, a referral, a LinkedIn post? That context tells you where they are in the buying journey. A referral who already knows what you do is a different conversation than cold outbound who is exploring for the first time.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">Who you are talking to.</strong>&nbsp;Are they the decision-maker or an influencer? Do they have the budget authority to say yes? Knowing this before the call shapes how you run the conversation.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left-color: rgb(232, 97, 10); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">"The quality of your discovery is directly proportional to the quality of your preparation."</blockquote><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">This is not a two-hour research project. Fifteen minutes of focused prep is enough to walk into the call with a real understanding of who you are talking to. That fifteen minutes is the difference between a call that moves a deal forward and one that goes nowhere.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">The Structure of a Strong Discovery Call</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A discovery call should not feel like an interview. It should feel like a real conversation between two people trying to figure out if they can help each other. But underneath that natural feel, there is a structure. And if you do not have one, the call will wander, run long, and end without the information you need.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Here's the structure I have used and taught for decades.</p><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 0px; border-radius: 10px; overflow: hidden; box-shadow: rgba(27, 58, 107, 0.1) 0px 4px 20px;"><svg style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Part 1: Set the Agenda (5 Minutes)</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The first thing you do on a discovery call is tell the prospect what you plan to do with the next thirty minutes. Not to show off your process. Because when people know what to expect, they relax and open up.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Something like this: "I want to use our time today to understand your situation, what's working, what's not, and where you are trying to go. At the end, I will give you my honest read on whether I can help and what that would look like. Fair enough?"</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">That last question - "Fair enough?" - matters. It is a mini-agreement. The prospect is now expecting to be asked questions, not pitched at.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Then ask if there is anything on their end they want to make sure you cover. Some of the best information I have ever gotten on a discovery call came from the prospect adding something to the agenda before the call even started.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">I want to tell you something that happens to me more than you might think. A founder gives me a few minutes of context about their business — what they sell, where they are stuck, what they have already tried. Then they stop and ask me directly: "Do you think you can help us?"</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">My answer is always the same.&nbsp;<em style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">I don't know. But we will find out.</em></p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Every time I say it, there is a brief pause. And then the founder almost always relaxes. Because they realize they are not talking to someone who is going to tell them what they want to hear. They are talking to someone who is actually going to look.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">That is the posture of a real discovery call. Not certainty. Honest curiosity.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Part 2: Understand Their World (5 Minutes)</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Before you can ask about problems, you need context. You already did your homework before the call. Now you confirm and build on it with a few short questions about the business, the team, and the current situation.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">This is not small talk and it is not filler. It is calibration. You are establishing the baseline so that when you start digging into problems, you know what normal looks like for this prospect and what the gap is.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Part 3: Find the Real Pain (15 Minutes)</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">This is the heart of the call. And this is where most salespeople move too fast.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">They hear a problem and they immediately start describing their solution. The prospect said they have a pipeline problem, and the rep launches into the features of their CRM. That is a mistake. The first problem a prospect describes is almost never the real problem. It is the surface layer. Underneath it is the thing that actually costs them money, burns them out, or keeps them from growing.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Your job is to drill until you get there. Not aggressively. Not like an interrogation. With genuine curiosity and follow-up questions that go deeper each time.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">"Tell me more about that." "How long has that been the case?" "What have you tried to fix it?" "What does that cost you — in time, in revenue, in stress?" "What happens if you do nothing about it for the next twelve months?"</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">That last question is one of the most valuable questions you can ask in any discovery call. Most prospects have not fully confronted the cost of inaction. When you ask them to do that math out loud, the urgency changes.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Part 4: Qualify the Deal (8 Minutes)</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">You need to know four things before you invest further time in any prospect. Budget. Authority. Need. Timeline. Most salespeople know this as BANT. Most salespeople also forget to actually ask these questions because the conversation felt good and they did not want to disrupt it.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Ask them anyway.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">You need to know who actually makes the decision to purchase. You need to know how decisions like this flow through their organization. You need to know whether they have a budget allocated or whether finding money is part of the work. And you need to know whether this is a problem they are solving in the next ninety days or next year.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">I learned something early in my career that still holds. If you get deep into the sales cycle without knowing how your order flows, you will find out at the worst possible moment. Ask it early. Ask it clearly. And keep drilling until you have the full picture.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Part 5: Close to the Next Step (7 Minutes)</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Every discovery call must end with a clear, agreed-upon next step. Not "I will send you some information." Not "Let me know if you have any questions." A specific action with a specific date attached.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Before you close, summarize what you heard. Not a word-for-word recap — a concise version of the problem, the cost, and the stakes. "Here is what I heard. You have been doing all the selling yourself for three years. You have tried to hire people but they have not stuck. You are burning out and you know it. And if nothing changes, you will not hit your growth target this year. Is that right?"</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">That summary does two things. It shows the prospect they were actually heard. And it re-creates the urgency that sometimes fades by the end of a conversation.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Then ask for the next step. Not "Does that sound helpful?" Ask for a specific action. "I want to put together a proposal based on what I heard today. Can we block thirty minutes next week to walk through it?"</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">The Questions That Actually Work</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The questions below are not a script. They are a starting point. Your ICP, your product, and the specific prospect will shape the conversation. But these are the questions I come back to over and over because they consistently unlock the information that moves deals forward.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">On the current situation:</strong></p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">"Walk me through how sales works at your company right now." "Who is doing most of the selling?" "What does your pipeline look like week to week?"</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">On the problem:</strong></p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">"What is the single biggest thing holding your sales back right now?" "How long has that been true?" "What have you already tried?" "Why did those attempts not work?"</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">On the cost:</strong></p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">"What does this problem cost you? In revenue, in time, in your own energy?" "What happens to the business if this is still the situation a year from now?" "If you solve this, what does that mean for your growth?"</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">On the decision:</strong></p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">"Who else needs to be part of this conversation before a decision gets made?" "How have you made decisions like this in the past?" "What does the approval process look like?"</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">On urgency:</strong></p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">"Is there a specific event or deadline that makes solving this time-sensitive?" "What happens if you push this to next quarter?"</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left-color: rgb(232, 97, 10); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">Stop counting features. Start counting follow-up questions. If you asked fewer than fifteen questions on your last discovery call, the conversation stayed on the surface.</blockquote><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 0px; border-radius: 10px; overflow: hidden; box-shadow: rgba(27, 58, 107, 0.1) 0px 4px 20px;"><svg viewBox="0 0 720 260" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" style="width:100%;display:block;"><text x="360" y="38" text-anchor="middle" font-family="Arial, sans-serif" font-size="15" font-weight="700" fill="#1B3A6B">The Talk/Listen Rule on a Discovery Call</text><text x="360" y="58" text-anchor="middle" font-family="Arial, sans-serif" font-size="11" fill="#5a6a84" letter-spacing="0.03em">YOU SHOULD BE TALKING 30% OF THE TIME. font-family="Arial, sans-serif" font-size="10.5" fill="#5a6a84">If you are talking more than your prospect, you are pitching. Stop and ask a question.</text></svg></div><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">The Mistakes That Kill Discovery Calls</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">I have watched talented salespeople lose deals they should have won because of errors that had nothing to do with price or product. Here are the ones I see most often.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Pitching before you have earned the right to pitch</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The moment you start describing your solution before you fully understand the prospect's problem, you have lost the high ground. The prospect stops feeling understood and starts feeling sold to. Those are very different experiences, and prospects can feel the difference immediately.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">There is a rule I use: the prospect must describe their problem to you at least three times in three different ways before you start talking about your solution. That sounds extreme until you realize that most salespeople start pitching after hearing it once.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Asking closed questions</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Closed questions get yes or no answers. Yes or no answers tell you almost nothing. "Do you have a sales process?" is a closed question. "Walk me through how your sales process works today" is an open question. The second one will get you three minutes of information that shapes everything you do next. The first one will get you a one-word answer and a conversation that goes nowhere.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Letting the call end without a next step</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A discovery call that ends with "I'll be in touch" is not a qualified opportunity. It is a pleasant conversation. There is a difference. The next step has to be specific, agreed to on the call, and calendared before the call ends. If you leave the next step to email, it will die in email.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Not asking about the decision process</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">You can run the best discovery call of your life and still lose the deal because you discovered the problem with the wrong person. Know who the financial buyer is. Know how orders flow. Know who else needs to be in the room. Find this out on the first call, not the fourth.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Accepting the first answer at face value</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Prospects give you the surface answer first. Every time. The real answer — the one with urgency, stakes, and budget attached — is three or four follow-up questions deeper. Most salespeople accept the first answer and move on. The best salespeople ask the next question.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">What to Do After the Call</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The discovery call does not end when you hang up. What you do in the next twenty-four hours determines whether the momentum you built on the call carries forward or evaporates.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Within the same business day, send a follow-up message that does three things. First, acknowledge the specific problem they described. Use their words, not yours. Second, confirm the next step you agreed to on the call, including the date and time. Third, attach any resource you promised.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Do not write a sales pitch in the follow-up. You will have time for that later. The follow-up should feel like a continuation of the conversation, not the start of a new one.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Also take five minutes immediately after the call to update your CRM with everything you learned. Not a summary. The specific answers to the key discovery questions: the problem, the cost, the decision process, the timeline, and the next step. If it is not in the CRM, it did not happen.</p><ul style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 0px; margin-bottom: 22px; margin-left: 0px; padding: 0px; list-style: none;"><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 8px 0px 8px 32px; position: relative; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237); line-height: 1.55;">Send a follow-up within 24 hours that confirms the next step with a specific date and time</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 8px 0px 8px 32px; position: relative; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237); line-height: 1.55;">Log the real problem, the cost of the problem, the decision-maker, and the timeline in your CRM</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 8px 0px 8px 32px; position: relative; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237); line-height: 1.55;">Note any open questions you still need answers to — and build them into the next conversation</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 8px 0px 8px 32px; position: relative; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237); line-height: 1.55;">If the prospect was not a good fit, document why and disqualify the opportunity now, not later</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 8px 0px 8px 32px; position: relative; border-bottom: none; line-height: 1.55;">If the prospect was a strong fit, move the opportunity to the correct pipeline stage immediately</li></ul><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">When to Walk Away</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Not every discovery call should result in a next step. Some prospects are not a fit. Some are not ready. Some do not actually have the problem they thought they had. Figuring that out on the first call is a win, not a failure.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">If you finish a discovery call and you cannot clearly answer these three questions, do not advance the opportunity:</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">What is the specific problem and how urgent is it? Who makes the decision and what does the approval process look like? Is there a budget and a realistic timeline to use it?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A discovery call with a prospect you cannot sell to is not wasted time. It is research. You learn something about the market, about objections, about how companies in this space think about the problem you solve. That has value. But do not put it in your pipeline as a real opportunity. Your pipeline is not a list of everyone you have had a conversation with. It is a list of qualified deals with a realistic path to closing.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left-color: rgb(232, 97, 10); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">Your time is your income. The fastest way to waste it is to spend it on prospects who were never going to buy.</blockquote><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><a href="https://louiebernstein.com/articles/what-is-fractional-sales-leader" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 20px 24px; border-radius: 8px; display: flex; align-items: center; gap: 12px; text-decoration-line: none;"><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;"><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 4px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700; letter-spacing: 0.1em; text-transform: uppercase; display: block;">Related Reading</span><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.35;">What Is a Fractional Sales Leader? The Capital-Efficient Model for Scaling Out of Founder-Led Sales →</span></div></a><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">Frequently Asked Questions</h2><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How long should a discovery call be?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Thirty to forty minutes is the right target for most B2B discovery calls. Shorter than that and you cannot go deep enough on the real problem. Longer than that and you risk losing the prospect's attention and drifting into territory that belongs in a later conversation. If you regularly need more than forty minutes to complete discovery, it usually means your questions are not targeted enough, or you are doing too much talking.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: What is the difference between a discovery call and a sales call?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">A discovery call is a diagnostic conversation. Your goal is to understand the prospect's situation, not to sell them anything. A sales call — sometimes called a closing call or proposal call — comes later, after you have earned the right to present a solution because you have fully understood the problem. Mixing the two up is the single most common mistake I see. If you are presenting your product on the first call, you are skipping discovery. That costs you deals.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How do I get a prospect to open up on a discovery call?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Set the agenda at the start. Tell them you are there to understand their situation, not to pitch. Then ask open-ended questions and get out of the way. The biggest mistake salespeople make is filling silence. When a prospect pauses after answering a question, they are often deciding whether to go deeper. If you jump in with the next question too fast, you cut off information you would have gotten if you had just waited five more seconds. Silence on a discovery call is not awkward. It is productive.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: What if the prospect turns the call into an interrogation of me?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Some prospects will try to flip the dynamic and spend the first twenty minutes grilling you on your company, your process, and your pricing. The answer is not to get defensive or to answer every question at length. Acknowledge the question, give a brief honest answer, and redirect. "Good question — I am happy to cover that in detail. Before I do, I want to make sure what I tell you is actually relevant to your situation. Can I ask you a couple of questions first?" Most prospects will accept that. It is a reasonable ask. And it gets the call back on track.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How do I ask about budget without making it awkward?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Stop treating it as awkward and it will stop feeling that way. Budget is a standard part of any business decision. If you have built enough trust in the first part of the call, you can ask it directly: "To make sure I am proposing something that makes sense for your situation — do you have a number in mind for what solving this problem is worth?" Or more directly: "Is there a budget range you are working with?" Some founders will deflect. That is useful information too. But most will answer honestly if the question comes from a place of genuinely trying to help, not trying to extract a number to negotiate against.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: What if I get to the end of the call and do not have all the information I need?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Schedule a second discovery call. There is no rule that says all your discovery has to happen in one conversation, especially for complex sales. What you should never do is advance the opportunity to a proposal stage before you have the full picture. A proposal built on incomplete discovery will miss the target. Better to book a second thirty-minute call to fill in the gaps than to send a proposal that does not address what the prospect actually needs.</p></div><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 48px 0px 32px; padding: 40px 36px; border-radius: 10px; text-align: center;"><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 12px; padding: 0px;">Your sales team is running discovery calls right now.</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 24px; padding: 0px;">The question is whether they are uncovering the real problem — or scratching the surface and calling it done.
