You earn it by running real discovery first. If you skip that step, you walk into the demo blind, and a blind demo always becomes a feature dump, because when you don't know what matters to them, you show everything and hope something sticks.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Before every demo, I get two things ready. The first is the room. The second is the plan.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Get the room ready</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Have your supporting materials open and one click away. Your pricing sheet, your service agreement, the specific screens you know they'll want to see. Ask permission to record the call so you can send it to the people who weren't there. Open a second screen or a viewer so you can see exactly what the prospect sees, because there's always lag and you never want to be the last one to notice something froze. And share only the application window, not your whole desktop with its inbox notifications and forty open tabs. Sloppy screen-sharing tells a buyer everything about how you'll handle their account.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Get the plan ready</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">You should already know, from discovery, what problem you're solving and who's in the room. Without one, you ramble, you run long, and you end without a next step. Here's the structure I've used and taught for decades. It's the same bones as a great discovery call, just aimed at proving the fix instead of finding the problem.</p><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 0px; border-radius: 10px; overflow: hidden; box-shadow: rgba(14, 77, 92, 0.1) 0px 4px 20px;"><img src="https://ckffpnbnpvwmecwzovxf.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/media/articles/1783980952044_demo-b2b-structure.png" alt="The 5-Step B2B Software Demo: 1) Set the Stage, get names and roles and ask what matters most; 2) Frame the Value with a 30-second pitch, then lead with what the senior buyer asked; 3) Run the Demo tailored, not a data dump, in short bursts; 4) Qualify Live by asking how purchase decisions get made; 5) Close to Next Step with 'what's the next step, then what happens?'" style="width:100%;display:block;border-radius:10px;" /></div><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 1: Set the Stage</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Get the names and responsibilities of everyone on the call. You need to know who the decision-maker is, who the user is, and who's there to poke holes. Then ask each person the single most useful question you can ask at the start of any demo: "Given our time today, what's the most important thing I could show you?"</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Write down every answer. Start the demo with content tied to what the highest-level person in the room told you they wanted to see. You're not building suspense. You're rewarding their attention immediately by proving you listened.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Right after that first piece of value, ask the senior person a real qualifying question: "How are decisions to purchase products like ours made in your company?" Even if you asked it in discovery, the answer often changes once the group is on the line. Ask it again.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 3: Run the Demo</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Now you work through the tailored path you built, adjusting to cover what each person asked for. This is the part where most people go wrong, so I'll be blunt. Do not narrate every click. Show a piece, stop, and ask a question. Show the next piece, stop, ask again.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Throughout the demo, pepper in the questions that keep it a conversation and keep qualifying: "What are you doing for this today?" "What's driving your interest in solving it now?" "Is this project budgeted?" That last one is a fair yes-or-no question by this point, and the answer tells you how real the deal is.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 4: Qualify Live</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A demo isn't just a show, it's your best chance to confirm this is a real opportunity while everyone's engaged. You need to leave knowing how the decision gets made, who signs, whether there's a budget, and what the timeline is. Don't save it for a follow-up email where it dies. Ask it live, while you've got the whole room.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">I learned this the hard way early in my career. If you get deep into a deal without knowing how the order actually flows through the company, you find out at the worst possible moment, usually the day it stalls. Ask early. Ask clearly.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 5: Close to the Next Step</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Conclude the demo and ask, "Did I cover what everyone wanted to see?" Once they say yes, turn to the decision-maker and ask, "What's the next step?" Let them answer. Then ask the most underused question in all of selling: "Then what happens?"</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Those two questions do the work. It's a nice conversation you'll never hear about again.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">The Feature Dump vs. The Value Demo</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">When Gong went through those 67,149 demos, the difference between winning and losing wasn't the product. The other is what closes.</p><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 0px; border-radius: 10px; overflow: hidden; box-shadow: rgba(14, 77, 92, 0.1) 0px 4px 20px;"><img src="https://ckffpnbnpvwmecwzovxf.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/media/articles/1783981018400_demo-b2b-compare.png" alt="The Feature Dump versus The Value Demo, based on Gong's study of 67,149 demos. In Gong's data, not one demo that led to a closed deal contained more than 76 seconds of uninterrupted talking. Not one. The winning demos had 21% more speaker switches per minute, meaning more back-and-forth, more of the prospect chiming in. If you're talking for five minutes straight, you're not demoing. You're lecturing, and the buyer checked out four minutes ago.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Sell the outcome, not the feature</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A feature is what the software does. Value is what changes in their business because of it. "This dashboard shows pipeline by stage" is a feature. "This is where you'll catch the deals that were about to slip, the week before they slip, instead of at the end of the quarter" is value. Same screen. Completely different sale. The buyer doesn't remember your feature list. They remember whether you understood their problem.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Spend real time on next steps</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The winning demos in Gong's study spent 12.7% more time discussing next steps at the end.
