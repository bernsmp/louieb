Then coach that, not the symptom.</text></svg></div><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Ego (the top-performer trap)</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The rep is hitting their number and they've decided that makes them untouchable. The fix isn't to argue with their numbers. The fix is to show them what's possible above the number. "You're at 110% of quota. The reps I've coached in this exact situation moved to 140%. Want to look at where the 30% lives?" Top performers respond to bigger targets, not to criticism.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Past burn</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A previous manager used coaching as a documentation tool to fire people. So now this rep hears the word "coaching" and braces for impact. You can't argue them out of it. You have to prove the new game is a different game. Coach them on something small and watch what happens. If the next week they come back and you reinforce what worked instead of digging at what didn't, the wall drops.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Comfort</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">This is the most dangerous one because it looks like success. The rep has a routine. The routine works. They're guarding the number, not building the skill. The fix is to attach coaching to a growth target they actually want. Bigger deals. A new segment. Promotion. If they don't want any of those, you have a different problem and you should know it.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Fear</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The rep knows a gap is there and doesn't want it named. Discovery is shallow. Forecasting is a guess. Their pipeline is built on hope. The resistance is the cover. You don't push through fear by force. You make the coaching environment safe enough that the rep names the gap themselves. Sometimes that's a one-on-one. Sometimes that's letting them watch a call you ran and asking what they noticed.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Bad framing (this one's on you)</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">If the only time you coach is when the numbers slip, you've trained the team to associate coaching with trouble. The fix is on you. Coach when things are going well. Coach the top performer first, in front of the team. Make coaching the most ordinary thing that happens in a week. Then the resistance disappears on its own because the word stopped meaning what it used to mean.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(232, 97, 10); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">"If the only time you coach is when the numbers are down, you've taught the team that coaching means trouble. Then you wonder why they resist."</blockquote><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">The Five-Step Coaching Loop That Works on Resistant Reps</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Once you've diagnosed why a rep is pushing back, you still need a system to run them through. The same five steps work on every type of resistance. The only thing that changes is the entry point.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 1. Set the standard up front</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Every rep on the team gets the same one-on-one cadence. Weekly. Not "if we have time." Not "when there's a problem." Weekly. The agenda is fixed. Pipeline review, one coaching topic, agreed next step. When coaching is on the calendar for everyone, no rep can interpret it as a personal indictment.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 2. Start small</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">One specific behavior. Not "your discovery is shallow." That's a verdict. Try: "On the call with Acme last Thursday, the prospect said they were 'looking at a few options.' You moved past it. What if you'd asked them which options and why?" Specific. Recent. About one moment. That's coachable.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 3. Ask, don't tell</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">"What did you hear when she said that?" beats "you should have said this." Every time. The rep arrives at the insight, which means they own the change. The minute you tell them the answer, they hear an order, not coaching. And resistant reps push back on orders harder than anyone.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 4. Document</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Two lines in the CRM or your one-on-one notes. What we coached on. What the rep agreed to do this week. Both sides see it. Nobody has to remember it. Documentation isn't bureaucracy. It's the fairness mechanism that protects the rep and the manager equally. If you're not writing it down, the conversation didn't happen.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 5. Inspect</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Next week's one-on-one opens with: "Last week we agreed you'd ask 'which options and why' on at least three calls. Walk me through how that went." If they did it, you reinforce. If they didn't, you have a real conversation, not a coaching conversation. The inspection step is the part founders skip the most, and it's the part that makes the whole loop work.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">If a rep won't engage with this loop, you don't have a coaching problem. You have a performance problem. And those, as I said, have different consequences. The Accountabilities Document you wrote when you hired them tells you exactly what those consequences are. Top performer two years running. Built his own book. Closed more revenue than anyone else on the team. He also refused to be coached. Said it more than once. "I'm hitting the number, Louie. Leave me alone."</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">I made the founder mistake. I let him. Because his numbers were good and I had a hundred other fires to put out. For about eight months I treated Mark like an exception.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Two things happened. First, Mark plateaued. Same revenue four quarters in a row. Not bad. Not growing either. Second, and this one was worse, the rest of the team noticed. Two of my mid-pack reps started pushing back when I tried to coach them. "Mark doesn't do this. Why do I have to?" That's when it hit me. By exempting Mark, I'd given every other rep permission to opt out too.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">I sat Mark down. I said something I should have said eight months earlier. "Coaching isn't optional here. Not for you. Not for anyone. You don't have to like it. You do have to show up to it. If that doesn't work for you, we should talk about whether this is still the right job."</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">He was furious for about a week. Then he showed up. Six months later he was at 145% of quota. Two of the mid-pack reps who'd been quietly opting out came back to the loop too. The team grew because I'd stopped letting one rep set the ceiling.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(232, 97, 10); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">"By exempting Mark, I'd given every other rep permission to opt out too. The ceiling on your team is whatever you tolerate from your top performer."</blockquote><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">How a Fractional Sales Leader Runs This Without the Founder Becoming the Bad Guy</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The hardest part of coaching reps who push back isn't the technique. It's the relational tax. You're the founder. You hired them. You may have closed deals together. Now you have to be the one who tells them their forecast is sloppy or their discovery is shallow. Every conversation has six years of context attached.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A Fractional Sales Leader takes that hat off you. Same standard. Same coaching loop. Different chair. The reps stop reading every piece of feedback as "the founder is mad at me" and start reading it as "this is how the team operates." That shift alone is worth the engagement.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">What changes inside the first ninety days</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A Fractional Sales Leader writes the Accountabilities Document if it doesn't exist. Builds the one-on-one cadence and shows up to every one of them. Coaches the top performer first, on purpose, so the rest of the team sees coaching is universal. Documents what was coached and what was agreed. Inspects it the next week. By day ninety, the team has stopped asking whether they have to be coached and started asking what they're being coached on.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">What the founder gets back</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">You get out of the seat where every coaching conversation is loaded. You stay the visionary, the strategist, the closer on the deals where the founder seat is the asset. You don't lose the relationship with the rep. You just stop being the one who has to break the news about their discovery on every Tuesday morning. That's a job. It's somebody else's job.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><a href="https://louiebernstein.com/articles/how-do-i-recruit-and-retain-top-sales-talent" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 20px 24px; border-radius: 8px; display: flex; align-items: center; gap: 12px; text-decoration-line: none; background: rgb(244, 246, 250); border-left: 4px solid rgb(27, 58, 107);"><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;"><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 4px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700; letter-spacing: 0.1em; text-transform: uppercase; display: block; color: rgb(27, 58, 107);">Related Reading</span><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.35; color: rgb(27, 58, 107);">How Do I Recruit and Retain Top Sales Talent? →</span></div></a><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">Frequently Asked Questions</h2><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: What if my top performer threatens to leave if I push coaching?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Then you have an answer about who that rep really is. The reps you actually want don't issue ultimatums about being coached. They ask for more of it. If your top performer would walk over a weekly one-on-one and a coaching loop, the relationship was already transactional. Don't run your team based on the threat. Run it based on the standard. Sometimes a rep walks. More often they don't, and the ones who stay produce more. Either way you're better off than you were running a team where coaching is optional.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How often should I actually coach each rep?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Weekly. Thirty minutes. Same time every week. The agenda has three parts. Pipeline review, one coaching topic, the agreed next step. If you're doing it less than weekly, you're not coaching, you're checking in. Checking in doesn't change behavior. Weekly cadence does. Founders push back on this because they're busy. The reps push back because they're not used to it. Both objections disappear once the loop is producing measurable results, which is usually about six weeks in.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: What's the difference between coaching and managing performance?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Coaching is about growth. Performance management is about whether somebody keeps the job. The coaching loop assumes the rep wants to get better. Performance management starts when the rep won't engage with the loop or won't deliver on what they agreed to. They're not the same conversation and they shouldn't feel the same. If you blur them together, your top performers will read every coaching session as a step toward being fired, and your underperformers will read every performance conversation as just more coaching. Keep them separate.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: My reps say they don't have time for coaching. What do I say?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">You say "we make time." Then you book the thirty minutes and you don't move it. The rep who tells you they don't have time for coaching is the same rep who'll spend three hours on a deal that was unqualified from the first call. Coaching is the highest-leverage thirty minutes in their week. It's not extra. It's the work. Frame it that way and stop letting it slide. If you let one rep cancel because they're busy, you've taught the whole team it's optional.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: What if the rep knows more about sales than I do?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">You're still the boss. Coaching isn't about you knowing more than the rep. It's about you holding the standard for the team. Some of the best coaches in any field weren't the best players. They were the people who saw the game from the side and asked the right questions. If you really feel out of your depth on the technique, that's exactly the moment to bring in a Fractional Sales Leader. Not to replace you. To handle the coaching while you handle vision, strategy, and the deals where your seat is the asset.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How do I coach without making the rep feel like I'm second-guessing them on every deal?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;">Coach the behavior, not the deal. There's a difference between "you shouldn't have offered the discount on Acme" and "what do you do when a prospect asks for a discount in the first call?" The first is a verdict on one transaction. The second is a skill they can take into every future deal. Resistant reps hate the first. They engage with the second. Same content. Completely different reception. The coaching frame is forward-facing and skill-based. The verdict frame is backward-facing and deal-specific. Use the first one until they ask for more.</p></div><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 48px 0px 32px; padding: 40px 36px; border-radius: 10px; text-align: center; background: rgb(244, 246, 250); border: 1px solid rgb(27, 58, 107);"><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 12px; padding: 0px; color: rgb(27, 58, 107);">Stop tolerating a coaching opt-out.</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 24px; padding: 0px; color: rgb(90, 106, 132);">Thirty minutes.
