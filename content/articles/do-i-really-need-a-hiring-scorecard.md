Without it, you are hiring on feel.</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 6px 0px 6px 22px; line-height: 1.55; position: relative;">Hiring on feel is how founders end up paying a $90,000 base salary to someone they "liked in the interview" who never books a meeting in their first ninety days.</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 6px 0px 6px 22px; line-height: 1.55; position: relative;">A good scorecard rates the candidate on the things that actually predict sales success: motivation, coachability, work ethic, resilience, and self-improvement.</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 6px 0px 6px 22px; line-height: 1.55; position: relative;">The scorecard is also your defense against your own bias. You will like candidates who are like you. The scorecard makes you score the ones you don't like, too.</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 6px 0px 6px 22px; line-height: 1.55; position: relative;"><b>Great salespeople are interviewing you as much as you're interviewing them. Can't I just trust my gut?"</b></p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">My answer is always the same. Yes, you need one. And no, you can't trust your gut. Your gut has hired the last two salespeople who didn't work out. Your gut is the reason you're calling me.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A hiring scorecard isn't a form. It isn't paperwork. It's the most important document in your company, because the people you hire are the people who will either build your revenue engine or break it. And right now, if you're like most of the founders I talk to, you don't have one.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Here's why that has to change before you make your next sales hire.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">What a Hiring Scorecard Actually Is</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A hiring scorecard is a one-page document. On the left side is a list of questions. In the middle is a rating column from 1 to 5. On the right is a place to write down what the candidate actually said. That's it. That's the whole tool.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">It sounds simple. It is simple. But the moment you put it in front of yourself before an interview, three things happen that don't happen otherwise.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong>You ask the same questions every time.</strong>&nbsp;Which means you can finally compare candidates against each other instead of comparing each one to your mood that day.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong>You write down the answers.</strong>&nbsp;Which means three weeks later, when you can't remember which candidate said what, you have a record. Not a vibe. A record.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;"><strong>You score them honestly.</strong>&nbsp;A 1 to 5 rating forces you to commit. The candidate either had a great answer or they didn't. "Pretty good" isn't on the scorecard. You have to pick a number.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(232, 97, 10); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">"If you don't write it down and score it, you're not interviewing. You're having a conversation. Those are different things."</blockquote><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Founders push back on this. They tell me they're good judges of people. They've been in business for years. They can tell within five minutes whether someone is going to work out.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">I always ask them the same follow-up. "How many of your last three sales hires worked out?" The answer is almost never three. It's usually one. Sometimes zero. style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Why these nine questions matter</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A founder I worked with last year had two candidates in the final round. One was a quieter, more methodical rep with a strong track record at a competitor. The other was a high-energy closer with a great pitch. Founder loved the high-energy one and was ready to make the offer.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Then the methodical rep asked the founder, "How many of your salespeople hit quota last quarter?" The founder didn't know. The rep asked, "Do you have an active Sales Playbook?" The founder said they had one but it was a few years old.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The rep was polite. Said thank you. Took an offer from another company that week.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The high-energy candidate took the job, didn't last six months, and the founder called me. The methodical rep who got away would have stayed for years. He was the one asking the right questions. The other one wasn't, and that should have been the tell.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(232, 97, 10); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">"A players ask hard questions. B players ask soft ones. C players just say 'when can I start?' Hire accordingly."</blockquote><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Question 5 is the one I watch most closely. Whether the founder has an active Sales Playbook is a direct signal of whether the company is ready for a salesperson at all. Good sales departments have an active Sales Playbook with documented processes and systems in place for onboarding, prospecting, qualifying, closing, and ongoing coaching. If you don't have one, you're not hiring a salesperson. You're hiring a survivor.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">Why This Document Matters More Than Your Comp Plan</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">I've sat across from hundreds of founders who spent weeks building a beautiful compensation plan with accelerators, SPIFFs, kickers, and tier breakpoints. The same founders interviewed their candidate over coffee and made the hire based on whether they liked the person.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">That's backwards. Your comp plan doesn't matter if you hired the wrong person. The most generous comp plan in your industry won't save a rep who can't handle rejection. Whether you're paying 8% or 12% on closed deals is irrelevant if the salesperson can't close.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The scorecard is the document that decides whether your comp plan ever gets used. It comes first. Always.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Here's the other thing the scorecard does that no other document in your company does: it protects you from yourself. You will like candidates who remind you of yourself. You will like candidates who laugh at your jokes. You will like candidates who flatter your product. None of those things predict whether they can sell.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">The scorecard forces you to rate them on what predicts performance. Motivation, coachability, work ethic, resilience, self-awareness. If they score a 2 on resilience, it doesn't matter how much you liked them. They will fold the first time a prospect ghosts them.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(232, 97, 10); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">"Liking someone isn't a hiring criterion. Score them. Then decide."</blockquote><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">How to Build Your Own Hiring Scorecard</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">You don't need anything fancy. A Google Doc or a single sheet of paper works fine. Here's what to do this week, before you talk to another candidate.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 1: List the 15 to 20 questions you'll ask every candidate</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Start with mine if you want. They've been refined over decades of hiring. Add or change three or four to make them yours. The point isn't perfection. The point is consistency. You ask the same questions of every candidate so you can compare answers.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 2: Build a 1 to 5 rating column next to every question</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A 1 means the answer was a red flag. A 3 means it was acceptable but not impressive. A 5 means it was the kind of answer that made you sit up. Don't overthink the scale. The discipline of picking a number is what matters.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 3: Leave a response column to write their actual words</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Don't paraphrase. Capture the candidate's words as close to verbatim as you can. Two weeks from now when you're comparing finalists, you'll need the exact answers, not your memory of them.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 4: Decide your threshold before you interview</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Before the first candidate walks in, decide what total score is a pass and what's a fail. For example, if your scorecard has 20 questions and the max is 100, decide that anyone below 70 doesn't move forward. Decide that anyone with a score below 3 on resilience or coachability is auto-disqualified regardless of total. Make these rules before you meet anyone, because once you like a candidate, you'll move the goalposts.</p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 10px; padding: 0px;">Step 5: Score immediately after the call</h3><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Not the next morning. Not after lunch. The moment the call ends, fill in the ratings while the answers are fresh. The longer you wait, the more your scores will drift toward your feeling about the candidate and away from what they actually said.</p><blockquote style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 28px; margin-bottom: 28px; padding: 14px 22px; border-left: 4px solid rgb(232, 97, 10); line-height: 1.65; border-radius: 0px 6px 6px 0px;">"The scorecard is a discipline, not a document. The document just makes the discipline possible."</blockquote><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Once you've done this five or six times, it becomes second nature. You walk into every interview prepared, you walk out with real data, and you make hiring decisions based on what the candidate actually said, not what you wished they'd said.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">What Happens When You Skip the Scorecard</h2><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">Founders who skip the scorecard tend to follow the same pattern. They have three or four pleasant conversations with candidates. They pick the one they liked best. They make the offer. The new rep starts. Ninety days in, the rep hasn't booked the meetings the founder expected. Six months in, the founder is wondering if the rep is going to make it. Nine months in, the rep is gone, the founder is back to running sales alone, and they're $90,000 to $200,000 lighter on payroll and severance with nothing to show for it.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">I've watched this movie dozens of times. The script never changes. The cost is always brutal. And the cause is almost always the same: the founder hired without a scorecard, on feel, on chemistry, on hope.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 22px; padding: 0px;">A Fractional Sales Leader builds the scorecard before you interview anyone. It's just discipline applied to a decision that's too important to wing.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 230, 237);"><a href="https://louiebernstein.com/articles/how-do-i-avoid-hiring-the-wrong-salesperson" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 40px 0px; padding: 20px 24px; border-radius: 8px; display: flex; align-items: center; gap: 12px; text-decoration-line: none;"><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;"><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 4px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700; letter-spacing: 0.1em; text-transform: uppercase; display: block;">Related Reading</span><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.35;">How Do I Avoid Hiring the Wrong Salesperson? →</span></div></a><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 14px; padding: 0px;">Frequently Asked Questions</h2><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px 0px 28px; border-bottom: 1px solid rgb(226, 230, 237);"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 8px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: Isn't a scorecard just bureaucratic?
