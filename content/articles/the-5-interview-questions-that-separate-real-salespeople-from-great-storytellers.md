They force specific evidence of past behavior rather than rehearsed theory. That evidence is the only reliable input for a candidate evaluation worth trusting.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 232, 240);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 16px; padding: 0px; letter-spacing: -0.015em;">Question 1: "Walk Me Through the Last Deal You Lost. margin: 0px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 600;">An Ideal Candidate Profile</span>&nbsp;— not a job posting, but a specific written profile of the person who will succeed in&nbsp;<em style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;">your</em>&nbsp;environment: your sales cycle length, deal complexity, prospecting requirements, and the culture they need to thrive in</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;"><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 600;">A structured interview process</span>&nbsp;— the same behavioral interview questions asked of every candidate in the same order, so your candidate evaluation is consistent and comparable</li><li style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.65;"><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 600;">A Sales Playbook</span>&nbsp;— a documented, proven and repeatable sales process your new hire can execute from Day 1, not puzzle out over 90 days at your expense</li></ul><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px;">Without all three, you are making a gut-feel decision on a $75,000 to $150,000 investment. That is not a hiring process. That is a coin flip with consequences.</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px;">A Fractional Sales Leader builds all three components of your sales hiring infrastructure before the first candidate walks through the door — so every hire you make from that point forward is a system decision, not a gamble.</p><hr style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 36px 0px; padding: 0px; border-right: none; border-bottom: none; border-left: none; border-image: initial; border-top-style: solid; border-top-color: rgb(226, 232, 240);"><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-top: 40px; margin-bottom: 16px; padding: 0px; letter-spacing: -0.015em;">Frequently Asked Questions</h2><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px;"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How many rounds of interviews should be part of my sales hiring process?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.7;">At minimum, three. A phone screen to qualify the basics — quota history, prospecting approach, reason for leaving. A full behavioral interview using the five questions above plus the role-play. And a final round with a second evaluator who will work alongside this rep. Never make a sales hire after a single conversation. The cost of a bad hire — typically 30% or more of first-year earnings plus lost pipeline — is too high to shortcut the process.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px;"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: Should I include a written skills test in my sales hiring process?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.7;">Yes, for full-cycle roles. Ask candidates to research your top two or three competitors and deliver a one-page battle card. This tests initiative, analytical thinking, and the preparation habit that top-performing sales reps bring to every prospect meeting. Reps who will not do a homework assignment for a job they want will not do the research your customers deserve.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px;"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: What if a candidate has impressive sales experience but performs poorly in the role-play?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.7;">Take the role-play result seriously. A strong resume earns the interview. The role-play tells you what happens when they are actually in front of a skeptical buyer. If they fall apart under mild pressure with you, they will fold in a real discovery call with someone who has three competitors on their shortlist.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px;"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: How do I get honest information out of reference checks?</p><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px; line-height: 1.7;">Ask one question: "On a scale of 1 to 10, how likely are you to rehire this person if you had an open role tomorrow?" Then ask: "What would need to be true for that number to be a 10?" That second question will tell you more about this candidate than anything they said in either interview. Top sales talent will always generate near-perfect answers — and this question surfaces the honest nuance behind the enthusiasm.</p></div><div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 28px; padding: 0px;"><p style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px 0px 20px; padding: 0px; font-weight: 700;">Q: I have no formal sales background.
