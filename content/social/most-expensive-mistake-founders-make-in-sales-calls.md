# LinkedIn Post

The most expensive mistake I see founders make on a sales call isn't the pitch. It isn't the close.

It's that they stop listening the second they think they know where the prospect is going.

While the buyer is still talking, the founder is already drafting the reply.

I get why. You know your product better than anyone alive. You've heard the question a hundred times. So your brain races ahead.

That confidence feels like competence. To the prospect, it reads as someone who already decided what they need and stopped paying attention.

Here's what it actually costs you.

You answer the objection you expected instead of the one they have. They hesitate on price, you launch your value defense, and it turns out they were worried about adoption, not cost. You just solved a problem they don't have.

You miss the buying signal they handed you. The deadline, the frustrated comment about a competitor, the name of the person who signs. Those are the exact words that close the deal, and they walk right past you.

And you lose the best question on the call. The one that comes from what they just said. It can't be scripted, so if you weren't listening, it never forms.

The fix isn't a new script. It's a discipline. Take notes instead of forming rebuttals. Pause a beat before you respond. Repeat the concern back and ask if you got it right.

The best salespeople aren't the ones with the smoothest answers. They're the ones who make the other person feel completely heard.
