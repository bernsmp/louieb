## LinkedIn Post

Most founders I work with tell me they have a Sales Playbook.

Almost none of them do.

They have a slide deck from a kickoff two years ago. They have a half-written Notion page. They have a Google Doc that nobody's opened since the rep who wrote it left the company.

That's not a playbook. That's an artifact.

Here's how I tell the difference in 30 seconds.

A real Sales Playbook can answer five questions specifically:

Who exactly do we sell to, and who do we refuse to sell to?

What buyer evidence has to exist for a deal to move from stage to stage?

What discovery questions does every rep ask, in what order, and what does a strong answer sound like?

What's the documented response to the top 10 objections our reps will hear this quarter?

When does the team sit down every week and actually use the playbook to review pipeline?

If your team can't answer those five with written content, you don't have a playbook. You have a hope that everyone's on the same page. They're not.

The reason founders skip building one isn't laziness. It's that building it feels like overhead when you're trying to hit a number. So they push it off. They run on instinct. They close deals personally. They assume they'll write it later.

Later never comes. By the time the team is big enough that the lack of a playbook is obviously hurting, it's a year too late and the bad habits are already baked in.

A real Sales Playbook isn't a document. It's the operating system for the sales team.

Build it first. Hire into it second. Then watch what happens when reps actually have something to follow.
