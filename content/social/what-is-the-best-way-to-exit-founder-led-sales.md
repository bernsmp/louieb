## LinkedIn Post (300–400 words)

Most founders try to exit founder-led sales by hiring. That is exactly why most founders never actually exit.

A new VP cannot inherit instinct. A new rep cannot read your mind. The pattern-matching that has lived in your head for years — what makes a deal real, when to push, when to walk — does not transfer through interviews or onboarding decks. It transfers through a written system. And the founders who never write down the system are the ones who end up back in every deal six months later, paying for a failed hire and exhausted on top of it.

The exit holds when you run it against a checklist. Five items. In order. No skipping.

One: codify your sales motion into an actual document. The playbook is the artifact that turns founder instinct into something teachable.

Two: hand off stage-by-stage. Top of funnel first. Close last. Founders who try to exit all at once watch revenue crater inside 60 days. Founders who hand off in sequence almost always succeed.

Three: install forecast discipline. If you cannot trust the CRM number without doing your own mental recount, you will not let go of the deals — and the exit will reverse.

Four: hire the Builder, not the Maintainer. A polished maintainer from a mature company will not survive your messy early-stage process. You need someone who has shipped revenue inside ambiguity before.

Five: run the witnessed handover. Ninety days where you are present but not driving. A board member, advisor, or fractional leader has to tell you when you slip back in. Without a witness, the team learns you are still the safety net.

The exit takes 90 to 180 days when run against a checklist. It takes 18 months or never when run on instinct. Pick one.

This is the work that decides whether your company outlasts the founder's calendar.
